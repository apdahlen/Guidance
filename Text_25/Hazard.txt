STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Kaldsret: not merely a generic “right to call,” but the congregation’s ecclesial right to call its own pastor or church servant. This is a technical free-church and Lutheran polity term and must remain strong and concrete.
* Ordinationsnægtelserne: literally “the refusals of ordination” or “ordinational refusals.” It refers not to abstract hesitation but to actual withholding of ordination for ecclesio-political reasons.
* Brøde: stronger than “error”; closer to “offense,” “wrong,” or “transgression.” It carries moral and ecclesial blame.
* Menighederne / Menighed: must be rendered consistently as “congregations” / “congregation,” not generalized into “church people,” since the polity argument depends on the concrete local congregation.
* Tilbøielighed: a bent, inclination, tendency rooted in fallen nature; stronger than a passing preference.
* ligger i Blodet hos os alle: an inherited human tendency; figurative, not biological in a modern scientific sense.
* kjødeligt Presteskab / kjødelige Prester: “carnal clergy” / “carnal pastors,” not merely “worldly.” The Pauline moral-theological force should be preserved.
* forurettede Menigheder: “wronged” or “injured” congregations, not just “affected.”
* ville herske over Herrens Arv: a scriptural expression; likely “lord it over the Lord’s heritage.” The biblical echo must remain visible.
* Frihedens skjønne Klenodi: literally “the fair/beautiful jewel of freedom.” The image should be retained.
* aandløse: literally “without spirit,” but here meaning spiritually lifeless or unspiritual in their understanding.
* Prestekonferentser: “pastoral conferences” or “clerical conferences,” but the secrecy and class-interest aspect must remain prominent.
* Standsinteresser: class interests, estate interests, guild interests; not merely “professional concerns.”
* Besættelserne: in context, church-office placements or imposed appointments, not demonic possession. Must be translated in a way that reflects imposed filling of pulpits.
* statskirkelig Orden / Ord: “state-church order” and “state-church ways/principle.” “Ord” here is not simply “word”; it denotes arrangement, order, custom, regime, or system.
* smugles ind: “smuggled in,” intentionally clandestine.
* indbilde: “make people imagine,” “persuade falsely,” “delude.”
* Prestesag / Biskophandling: a priestly matter / an episcopal act. The contrast is with the congregation’s right and competence.
* Knibe: a tight spot, bind, predicament.
* Forhaanelse: affront, insult, humiliation.
* oplyste: enlightened or instructed, specifically instructed in God’s Word and Christian order.
* katholske Overtro: “Catholic superstition” in the polemical 19th-century Lutheran sense. This must be rendered accurately without softening the confessional polemic.
* Indvielse: consecration/ordination/installation. In the quoted passage it must be handled carefully so that the church-order sense is preserved.
* kraftig ifølge guddommelig Ret: valid/effective by divine right.
* befiffede Biskoper: likely a typographical/OCR corruption of “beskikkede” or similar in the source tradition; context suggests bishops in office. In translation, the sense required is “bishops” as officeholders who persecute the Gospel and refuse ordination.
* Føie: just cause, warrant, proper ground.
* Uvæsen: abusive practice, disorder, mischief, evil business; stronger than “trouble.”
* Trældom: bondage, servitude.
* Standpunkt: position, stance.
* frikirkelige Menighedsfriheds Grund: the ground/principle/foundation of free-church congregational liberty.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long periodic sentences accumulate moral argument and rhetorical pressure. They must not be broken up excessively.
* Repeated “og da … og da … saa …” structures build inevitability and should be preserved.
* Rhetorical questions are not ornamental; they expose corruption and force assent.
* Semicolon logic is central. Clauses often pivot by contrast or escalation after a semicolon.
* The prose moves from general anthropological principle to concrete ecclesial abuse, then to confessional proof, then to warning. That progression must remain intact.
* Quoted material from church documents is embedded inside the argument and functions as decisive doctrinal evidence.
* Short exclamatory transitions such as “Ja se det er Veien, det gaar” carry oral force and should not be flattened.

Theological Hazards

* The contrast between “carnal” and “spiritual” is theological, not merely ethical or temperamental.
* The congregation is treated as a bearer of real ecclesial authority under God’s Word, not as an advisory body.
* Ordination is argued to be a Christian ecclesial act, not an ontologically exclusive act of bishops.
* “Divine right” must remain explicit, since the argument depends on what belongs to the church by divine institution.
* The polemic against episcopal refusal is grounded in Lutheran confessional teaching, especially the Smalcald Articles.
* “Where the Church is, there is also the command to preach the Gospel” is a doctrinal axiom and must retain full force.
* The right to call, choose, and ordain church servants is presented as belonging to congregations where bishops refuse the Gospel.
* “Ignorance, superstition, and bondage” are not rhetorical excesses but spiritual conditions on which corrupt church order is wrongly built.

Voice Hazards

* Sverdrup’s tone is solemn, sharp, polemical, pastoral, and unsentimental.
* The prose directly implicates readers and congregations in vigilance or negligence.
* Irony appears in quotation marks around terms like “good,” “fat,” and “filled,” and must remain visible.
* The voice combines moral seriousness with institutional critique; smoothing it into neutral academic prose would destroy the effect.
* The warning against clerical domination must sound like a living ecclesial danger, not a historical curiosity.
* The concluding admonition to the United Church must remain firm and prophetic, not merely advisory.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* ligger i Blodet hos os alle: means that this tendency belongs deeply to fallen human nature. A literal rendering is possible if kept idiomatic in English: “lies in the blood of us all” is too foreign; “is in the blood of us all” or “runs in us all” conveys the point.
* ville herske over Herrens Arv: biblical and pastoral expression, likely echoing 1 Peter 5:3. A natural equivalent is “lord it over the Lord’s heritage” or “lord it over the Lord’s flock/inheritance.” “Heritage” preserves the older resonance best.
* Frihedens skjønne Klenodi: image of liberty as a beautiful jewel or treasure. Literal preservation strengthens the rhetoric.
* mange krogede Veie: “many crooked ways,” meaning indirect and manipulative methods. The moral judgment in “crooked” should remain.
* den samme bitre Rod: likely biblical echo of the “root of bitterness” in Hebrews 12:15. This should remain visible.
* Ja se det er Veien, det gaar: idiomatic oral transition meaning “Yes, that is the way things go” or “That is how it goes.” It should keep its spoken, emphatic quality.
* bent imod Guds Ord: “straight against God’s Word,” not merely “contrary to.” The adverb intensifies open contradiction.
* komne ind i en Knibe: “come into a bind” or “gotten into a predicament.” The phrase implies entrapment.
* en liden Smule oplyste: modest phrasing with sharp implication—if congregations are even slightly instructed, they should know better.
* hævet over al Tvivl: “placed beyond all doubt,” a formula of doctrinal certainty.
* al Ret og Føie: paired expression meaning full right and proper warrant/grounds.
* Saadant Uvæsen gaar til en Tid: such abusive disorder can go on for a time. “Uvæsen” should not be weakened.
* tage klart og greit Standpunkt: “take a clear and straightforward stand.” The phrase has moral and ecclesial decisiveness.

Biblical allusions and pietistic expressions

* “lord it over the Lord’s heritage” strongly recalls 1 Peter 5:3 and should retain scriptural familiarity.
* “the same bitter root” likely echoes Hebrews 12:15.
* The contrast between fleshly/carnal and spiritual belongs to Pauline vocabulary.
* “Where the Church is, there is also the command to preach the Gospel” echoes confessional and scriptural ecclesiology.
* “Ignorance, superstition, and bondage” resonates with Protestant anti-hierarchical polemic and spiritual captivity language.

King James-style equivalents to consider, while avoiding archaic pronouns

* “lord it over the Lord’s heritage” is an especially natural inherited biblical equivalent.
* “beyond all doubt” works for “hævet over al Tvivl.”
* “divine right” is the natural confessional-ecclesial rendering for “guddommelig Ret.”
* “the Gospel” should remain capitalized and doctrinally weighty.

Expressions where literal translation would obscure force

* “Statskirkelig Ord” cannot simply be “state-church word”; it should be rendered as “state-church principle,” “state-church rule,” or “state-church system,” depending on syntax.
* “Besættelserne” cannot be translated literally as “occupations” or “possessions.” The sense is imposed appointments or placements.
* “aandløse” should not be “breathless.” It means spiritually lifeless in this context.
* “forurettede” should not be a bland “concerned.” It is “wronged.”

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis: To deprive congregations of their right to call is an old offense in the Church and recurs whenever opportunity arises.
* Anthropological explanation: Human beings resent the freedom of others when it limits their own advantage.
* Concrete application: A carnal clergy would gladly arrange matters so that congregations are reduced to benefices to be divided among clergy themselves.
* Pastoral warning: Because many pastors are carnal, and none are beyond temptation, congregations must remain watchful lest they lose their liberty.
* Symptom analysis: Spiritually lifeless clergy will show themselves through secret clerical conferences and attempts to dictate pastoral calls.
* Practical examples: Mission congregations are urged to surrender their call-right; independent congregations are steered toward elite clerical advice; disfavored candidates are blocked.
* Naming the problem in the United Church: “Appointments” and “refusals of ordination” are two manifestations of the same bitter root.
* Documentary evidence: An example from a western congregation thanks the mission committee for “appointment” and oversight of mission pastors.
* Judgment: State-church order is being smuggled in against both God’s Word and congregational liberty.
* Parallel case: Ordination refusals likewise begin by persuading people that ordination belongs only to priests or bishops, after which ordination is denied on church-political grounds.
* Deeper charge: This insults congregations by denying their capacity to understand God’s Word and Christian order.
* Confessional proof: The Smalcald Articles teach that there is no divinely instituted difference between bishops and pastors/priests and that congregations may ordain when bishops persecute the Gospel and refuse capable men.
* Contrast with former Lutheran freedom: Earlier Lutheranism was broad-minded on this point; now men claim the right to refuse ordination and criminalize those who proceed anyway.
* Exposure of strategy: First cultivate superstition, then use it as a chain upon congregations so they fear to exercise their call-right.
* Final warning and conclusion: Such abuse can continue for a time among dead and sleeping people, but building church bodies on ignorance, superstition, and bondage is dangerous. The United Church harms itself unless it stands clearly on the ground of free-church congregational liberty and fully respects the congregations’ right to call.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* “ville herske over Herrens Arv” likely echoes 1 Peter 5:3: not lording it over those entrusted to you / the Lord’s heritage.
* “den samme bitre Rod” likely echoes Hebrews 12:15, the “root of bitterness.”
* “kjødeligt” / “Aand” / “aandløse” echo Pauline flesh-versus-Spirit categories, especially Romans 8 and Galatians 5, though not a direct quotation.
* “Hvor Kirken er, der er ogsaa Befalingen til at prædike Evangeliet” is from Lutheran confessional logic, closely tied to the marks and authority of the Church.
* “kalde, vælge og indvie Kirketjenere” reflects ecclesial authority language rooted in both confessional and New Testament church order discourse.
* “døde og sovende Folk” may echo biblical images of spiritual sleep or deadness, though here it functions more generally as revivalist language.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings must remain warnings. The text speaks against real ecclesial corruption, not minor administrative mistakes.
* Rebuke of clerical self-interest must remain sharp.
* Irony in quoted terms must stay visible, since it exposes the corruption of the system.
* The prose should retain its prosecutorial pressure: it argues, exposes, and warns.
* The closing admonition to the United Church must sound like a grave judgment on its present course.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “trænges det bestandig” must remain necessity: “it is continually necessary,” not merely “helpful.”
* “skal miste” and similar forms should often remain “shall” or “are to,” depending on English rhythm and force.
* “vil” often expresses predictable consequence, not mere future tense. It should often be rendered as “will” in the strong sense of inevitability.
* “maa” in the confessional quotation must remain “must,” not “may,” where obligation or necessity is intended.
* “kan” and “kan ei” should preserve possibility/impossibility with full force.
* “vil man have Ret” should remain a claim to entitlement or asserted right.

* Prest / Prester / Presteskab: pastor / pastors / clergy, with “clergy” preferred for collective polemical passages

* kristelig Orden: Christian order

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* “the beautiful jewel of liberty” should remain metaphorical.
* “the same bitter root” should remain intact.
* “smuggled in” preserves the clandestine image of alien church order entering the church.
* “a chain upon the congregations” for “et Baand paa Menighederne” should remain concrete.
* “dead and sleeping people” should remain vivid, not paraphrased into “spiritually passive people.”

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The opening is analytical and historical.
* The middle intensifies into satire, rhetorical questioning, and institutional accusation.
* The confessional quotation shifts into formal doctrinal authority.
* The final paragraphs move back into warning and prophetic admonition.
* These tonal movements must remain visible and not be leveled into one uniform register.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Many sentences are designed for oral delivery, with accumulating subordinate clauses and delayed judgment.
* Repetition of “and” clauses should often be preserved to maintain sermonic breath and pressure.
* Brief oral interjections such as “Yes, that is the way it goes” should remain audible as speech.
* The quotation from the Smalcald Articles should sound declarative and public, as a doctrinal reading before an assembly.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

The Congregations’ Right to Call and the Refusals of Ordination

[“Folkebladet,” February 6, 1895.]

To deprive the congregations of their right to call is so old an offense within the Church that it is no wonder if it returns again whenever there is occasion.

For there is a tendency, which is in the blood of us all, that the freedom of others is hard to endure, if in any way it restricts our own and runs counter to our personal advantage.

If a tightly united, carnal clergy could get matters arranged in such a way that congregation was merged into pastorate and these pastorates were “good” or “fat,” and then the pastors could divide them among themselves, without these wronged congregations having anything to do with the matter, is there any doubt that all carnal pastors would find such an arrangement exceedingly good?

And since there are always many purely carnal pastors, and since all pastors without exception are not exclusively Spirit and therefore have their temptations to lord it over the Lord’s heritage, it is continually necessary that the congregations be on their guard, lest they lose the beautiful jewel of liberty and become a livelihood for those who desire to divide the offices among themselves.

Where the pastors have become generally spiritually lifeless in their understanding of the congregation and of their position in it, this will make itself known in various ways. Secret pastoral conferences will be formed and maintained, where the class interests of the clergy may be advanced; secret pastoral conferences have never been anything else, nor can they become it.

Next, the various attempts will be made to dictate to the congregations which pastor they are to have and not have. If it is a mission congregation, it is advised to surrender the right to call to the mission committee; if it is a more independent congregation, then it is to “apply to the chairman” and get his advice. If the congregation in particular wishes to have a man with whom the secret council is not pleased, then it is said: “No, he is not to be had; there is no use in calling him.” In short, there are many crooked ways by which the congregations’ free right to call is placed in danger once a carnal mind and machine politics first creep into the Church.

In the United Church we see things clearly enough. On the one side there are the “appointments”; on the other side there are the “refusals of ordination.” Both are the fruit of the same bitter root.

There is more than one mission congregation which has been advised to surrender its right to call to the mission committee, and there is more than one congregation which has followed the advice and has been “appointed over.” In the “Luth. Kirkeblad” we even find the following from a congregation in the far West:

“Likewise we thank the mission committee for its labor with the appointment and oversight of mission pastors, and with prayer that they will as soon as possible send us a pastor again.” (“L. K.” 1895, p. 26.)

Yes, that is the way it goes. State-church order and state-church rule are smuggled in upon the congregations straight against God’s Word and the freedom and right of the congregations.

Likewise with the refusals of ordination; first people are made to imagine that ordination is a priestly matter or an episcopal act, and then ordination is denied on purely church-political grounds, and then the congregations are to discover that they have come into a predicament from which they cannot get out.

It is first an affront to the congregations’ understanding of God’s Word and Christian order. For where the congregations are the least little bit instructed, they no longer have the Catholic superstition that ordination is an act of the assembled clergy, but rather a Christian act which can be carried out by any Christian congregation, just as the whole Lutheran Church unanimously confesses in the Smalcald Articles, where we read the following words, which may also have their application to our own circumstances with suitable changes.

“But since by divine right there is now no difference between bishops, pastors, or priests, it is beyond all doubt that when a pastor in his congregation ordains capable persons to church offices, then this ordination is valid according to divine right. Therefore, when the bishops persecute the Gospel and refuse to ordain capable men, then in such a case every congregation has full right and warrant to ordain its own church servants. For where the Church is, there is also the command to preach the Gospel. Therefore the congregations must retain the power to call, choose, and ordain church servants.”

So broad-minded one once was in the Lutheran Church; but now men will have the right to “refuse to ordain capable men,” and those who ordain them they will make into rebels and transgressors of the law. First they will make people superstitious, and then use the superstition as a chain upon the congregations, so that they shall not dare to exercise their right to call for fear of not getting their called pastor ordained.

Such abuse goes on for a time, especially among dead and sleeping people. We know, however, that it is dangerous work to build church bodies upon ignorance, superstition, and bondage. The United Church will find that it harms itself by not taking a clear and straightforward stand upon the ground of free-church congregational liberty and fully respecting the congregations’ right to call.

[Georg Sverdrup.]
