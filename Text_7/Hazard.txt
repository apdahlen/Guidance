STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* "Samfundsbegreber" does not mean merely "social concepts" here; in this ecclesial-polemical context it means conceptions or understandings of church fellowship / ecclesiastical association.
* "foreningen" refers to the church union / unification movement, not a generic association.
* "Den for. Kirke" = "Den forenede Kirke"; this abbreviation must be expanded mentally but not overexplained in the translation.
* "satte i Omløb" means "set circulating," "put into circulation," not simply "said."
* "vinde Hævdret" means gain accepted standing or prescriptive force by usage; not merely "become popular."
* "menighedsfarlige" means dangerous to the congregation, harmful to congregational life.
* "ad Snigvei" means by stealth, surreptitiously, by a side way; the polemical sting must remain.
* "privat Oppositionsskole" does not just mean a privately owned school; it means a private school set up in opposition, with an insinuation of illegitimacy.
* "fungerede som theologisk Professor" here implies served in office, exercised the function; not only "worked as."
* "resigneret" means resigned from office, not simply given up in a vague sense.
* "idethele" means altogether / in any sense, intensifying the polemic.
* "trofkyldige" is ironic: innocent, naïve, simple-hearted; here it is not praise but cutting irony.
* "Anskuelser" means views, conceptions, not just opinions.
* "exalterede Begreb" means exaggerated / overstrained concept, not spiritually exalted.
* "katholiserende" means tending toward Catholicizing, i.e. toward hierarchical-sacral church polity.
* "menighedsstridigt" means hostile to the congregation / contrary to congregational order.
* "omsætter" in this context means puts into effect, translates into practice, exploits in operation.
* "Samfundet" is a key doctrinal-polemical term: not society in the modern sociological sense, but church fellowship, ecclesiastical body, association of congregations.
* "den høiere Form af Menigheden" must retain the conceptual claim: a higher form of the congregation.
* "Aabenbarelse" here means manifestation, visible expression, not special revelation.
* "Sektvæsen" means sectarianism / sect-life / sectarian existence; literal "sect-being" would be awkward.
* "høihellig Ting" means a highly sacred thing; tone must remain ironic yet serious.
* "tilraads" means for counsel, for consultation.
* "frie Forholde" means free conditions / free circumstances, i.e. non-established-church conditions in America.
* "gjensidig Overenskomst" means mutual agreement, voluntary compact.
* "Gjøremaal" means practical undertakings / business / tasks.
* "forholdsmesigt større Udbytte" means proportionately greater benefit.
* "Grundenhed" means fundamental unit / basic unit.
* "anmasse sig Retten" means arrogate to itself the right.
* "Selvstændighed" means self-government / independence, not modern self-esteem.
* "kristelige Frihed" means Christian liberty.
* "prestelige Standsinteresser" means clerical class interests / estate interests.
* "frembringe Biskopembedet" means bring forth / produce the episcopal office.
* "Tarv" means need, welfare, spiritual good; an older morally weighted term.
* "Hensigtsmæssighedsforanstaltning" means expedient arrangement / measure of practical expediency.
* "Indretning" means institution / arrangement.
* "Splittelse, Sønderrivelse og Synd" is a triadic accusation with escalating moral force; it must not be flattened.
* "Opbyggelse" has devotional-theological force: edification, spiritual upbuilding.
* "pavemæssigt Overgreb" means papal / pope-like overreach.
* "fortrædige" here means hinder, obstruct, molest, trouble in the exercise of a right.
* "uantastelige Ret" means inviolable / unassailable right.
* "majoritetsgal" means crazed by majority-power, intoxicated with majority status; strongly polemical.
* "kontraherende Parter" means contracting parties.
* "udisputerlige" means indisputable.
* "tilfredsstillende" here means satisfactory in ecclesial principle, not merely emotionally pleasing.
* "paatide" means timely, called for, advisable now.
* "nedstemmer sine høie Toner" means lower / moderate their high tones; idiomatically, tone down their lofty language.
* "samfundsberettiget Tilværelse" means a fully societally / corporately legitimate existence; here the meaning is ecclesiastically recognized standing.
* "under Menighedens eller Kristi Legems Lov" means under the law/order of the congregation or Body of Christ.
* "Inkorporation" is legal incorporation under civil law.
* "Anstalt" here means institution.
* "Nøgternhed" means sobriety, level-headedness.
* "frugtbar Fortid" means fruitful past.

Syntactic Hazards

* The prose uses long cumulative sentences with multiple subordinate clauses; breaking them into short modern sentences would dissolve the argumentative pressure.
* Parenthetical insertions marked by dashes carry polemical force and must be preserved.
* Semicolons and commas organize logic, not merely breath; they often mark doctrinal pivots.
* Repeated "at" clauses build accusation and inference in layers; these must remain structurally legible in English.
* Contrastive questions ("Men er Samfundet ...?") are sermonic and must preserve the turn from claim to rebuttal.
* The author often states an opposing view in order to expose it; translation must keep clear whose voice is speaking.
* The passage moves from description to principle to application to rebuke; syntactic flattening would blur that movement.
* Some sentences rely on delayed main verbs and suspended resolution; the cadence of accumulation should be retained where possible.

Theological Hazards

* "kirkeligt Samfund" must not be translated in a way that suggests the mystical Church itself rather than a voluntary association of congregations.
* "Menigheden" is central and theological: congregation as the fundamental ecclesial unit under God’s Word.
* "den ene Kirke" invokes the doctrine of the one Church; it must remain doctrinally clear.
* The contrast between congregation and clerically dominated church-body is ecclesiological, not merely administrative.
* "Guds Ord" must remain as "God’s Word," preserving Lutheran doctrinal authority.
* "Kirken" versus "kirkesamfund" is crucial; the author rejects identifying any external association with the Church as such.
* "kristelige Frihed" carries Reformation resonance: Christian freedom over against coercive hierarchy.
* "aandelige Trang" means spiritual need or hunger; not general preference.
* "Opbyggelse" has theologically positive content: mutual edification in faith.
* "Katholicisme" here is not a broad slur against all catholicity, but polemically names hierarchical sacerdotal encroachment.
* "Kristi Legems Lov" evokes the Church as the Body of Christ and its divinely ordered life.

Voice Hazards

* The article is sharp, polemical, ironic, and ecclesiastically combative; smoothing would falsify the author’s voice.
* Irony appears in expressions such as "trofkyldige Udtalelser," "høihellig Ting," and "ikke er blevet majoritetsgal." These must retain their bite.
* The tone alternates between doctrinal instruction and satirical chastisement; both registers are deliberate.
* The author speaks with solemn certainty about congregational right and clerical overreach; this confidence must remain.
* The closing paragraphs sharpen from analysis into ridicule; that rhetorical escalation must be preserved.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "satte i Omløb" = put into circulation. This suggests not accidental misunderstanding alone, but active dissemination of ideas.
* "vinde Hævdret" = gain accepted standing by use or custom. The phrase implies that repetition may falsely establish a harmful notion as normative.
* "ad Snigvei" = by stealth / surreptitiously. It carries the sense of smuggling in a claim rather than openly arguing it.
* "kaste et skarpt Lys over" = cast a sharp light upon. This is a common rhetorical idiom and works naturally in English.
* "staa om" / "staar om" in citation form means "it says of." It introduces a quoted representation.
* "bortseet fra" = apart from / leaving aside. It brackets a secondary insult to focus on the deeper error.
* "over al Rimelighed exalterede Begreb" = a notion exaggerated beyond all reason. Literal translation is possible, but English should avoid sounding merely emotional.
* "med al Flid dyrker og omsætter" = diligently cultivate and put into effect. The image suggests a doctrine intentionally nurtured and operationalized.
* "gjør det identisk med ‘Kirken’" = makes it identical with "the Church." This is a theological overreach, not a lexical quibble.
* "alt udenfor som Sektvæsen" = everything outside as sectarianism / sectarian existence. This frames outsiders as illegitimate Christian bodies.
* "i det mindste" = in the least. Used to dismiss the claim that practical action changes the principle.
* "fri gjensidig Overenskomst" = free mutual agreement. This is nearly contractual language; it undergirds the ecclesiology of voluntary association.
* "Tallet gjør intet til Sagen" = the number makes no difference to the matter. The principle holds regardless of scale.
* "gjenstand for Forhandling" = a subject for negotiation. This idiom belongs to deliberative, corporate discourse.
* "nedstemmer sine høie Toner" = tone down their high tones / lofty language. English "tone down" captures the sense naturally.
* "majoritetsgal" = crazed by the majority. Literal rendering preserves the sting better than a softened paraphrase like "overconfident."

Biblical Allusions, Pietistic Expressions, and Scriptural Context

* "Menigheden" as the Church’s fundamental unit reflects New Testament congregational usage and free-church Lutheran ecclesiology.
* "efter Guds Ord" grounds the argument in Scripture as normative authority.
* "Opbyggelse" has strong biblical-pietistic resonance, corresponding to edification in Pauline ecclesial language.
* "aandelige Kræfter" and "aandelig Hjælp" belong to devotional-proclamation idiom, denoting spiritual resources, not merely morale.
* "Kristi Legems Lov" echoes Pauline Body-of-Christ imagery; there may not be a fixed verse quotation, but the conceptual field is scriptural.

Where the King James Bible offers a natural English equivalent

* "God’s Word" is already standard and appropriate.
* "edification" can serve for "Opbyggelse," since it preserves biblical-theological resonance.
* "the Body of Christ" is the natural scriptural equivalent for "Kristi Legeme."
* "Christian liberty" is a historically and theologically apt equivalent for "kristelige Frihed."

Expressions where literal translation would obscure force

* "Samfund" as "society" would mislead; "association," "fellowship," or "church body" depending on context is needed.
* "Anstalt" as "establishment" may sound too broad; "institution" is better here.
* "Tarv" as "want" would sound archaic in the wrong way; "welfare" or "good" or "need" depending on context carries the sense better.
* "fortrædige" literally suggests doing annoyance or harm; in context "hinder" or "obstruct" best conveys the legal-ecclesial force.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening thesis: the article will dwell on misunderstandings of the concept "church fellowship / ecclesiastical association" that are circulating within part of the United Church and threatening to gain accepted standing.
* Illustrative evidence: two recent statements are cited to expose the harmful assumptions—Augsburg called a "private" opposition school, and Prof. Oftedal / Sverdrup described as no longer theological professors.
* Interpretation of the evidence: these expressions rest on the idea that if Augsburg is no longer the United Church’s seminary, it becomes merely private, and that professors who resign from office in that body cease to be theological professors altogether.
* Deeper diagnosis: such views arise partly from an exaggerated conception of the United Church’s importance, but ultimately from a Catholicizing, anti-congregational concept of church fellowship promoted by clergy of the old synodical type.
* Definition of the opposing error: they treat the fellowship/body as a higher form of the congregation, identify their own body with the Church, and brand everything outside it as sectarian.
* Rebuttal by principle: no, the congregation does not disappear into the church body; even arbitrary clerical acts cannot alter that principle.
* Positive ecclesiological definition: under free conditions, a church fellowship is nothing more than a free mutual compact among congregations for cooperation in shared tasks.
* Doctrinal grounding: the congregation alone is, according to God’s Word, the Church’s basic unit; no church body may simply identify itself as "the Church."
* Warning: the Catholicizing concept damages congregational independence and Christian liberty, serves clerical interests, tends toward episcopal office, and leads back toward state-churchliness and dead orthodoxy.
* Practical implication: because such associations are expedient arrangements, they are not sacred structures from which departure is sinful.
* Assertion of right: if a congregation enters freely and is disappointed, it has the unassailable right to withdraw whenever it judges it beneficial.
* Further warning: any clergy who obstruct this right take a direct step toward Catholicism.
* Application to the present dispute: therefore it is absurd to call Augsburg "private" or deny the professors’ status, especially since the United Church has not yet formed a complete association.
* Historical/legal clarification: in 1890 the body took a first significant step toward union, but important conditions were attached and not fulfilled; one stipulation, the common seminary, was even broken by the majority leaders.
* Consequence: therefore no full union exists yet, and any party may withdraw from the union work at this stage without causing schism or constituting formal withdrawal.
* Forward-looking concession: there can only be talk of withdrawing until a more satisfactory form of union might become negotiable.
* Final rebuke: the majority leaders should tone down their lofty claims; the United Church is not identical with the United States nor with the Church.
* Legal demystification: its only fully legitimate existence is by civil incorporation, not by a law of the congregation or Body of Christ.
* Closing satirical thrust: even older church bodies should show sobriety and modesty; lack of these becomes ridiculous in a body not yet fully united after two years and in a seminary only days old.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* "efter Guds Ord Menigheden alene" echoes the Reformation appeal to Scripture as the norm for church order. It is not a direct quotation, but a scriptural principle claim.
* "Kirken" versus visible human associations echoes New Testament distinctions between the Church as Christ’s people and contingent outward organizations.
* "kristelige Frihed" resonates with Pauline and Lutheran language of Christian liberty, especially Galatians-informed Reformation discourse.
* "aandelig Hjælp" and "gjensidig Opbyggelse" echo Pauline notions of mutual edification within the body of believers (for example Romans 14; 1 Corinthians 14; Ephesians 4 conceptually).
* "Kristi Legems Lov" likely echoes Pauline Body-of-Christ theology (especially 1 Corinthians 12; Romans 12; Ephesians 4), though the phrase itself is not a direct biblical citation.
* "den ene Kirke" recalls confessional and scriptural language concerning the one Church, with conceptual resonance from Ephesians 4:4-6.

* The rebuke of false sacralization of church bodies must retain its indignation.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Concepts of Church Fellowship and the Union
in 1890.

[Editorial article in “Folkebladet” for September 20, 1893.]

We shall dwell here upon those misunderstandings—intentional or unintentional—of the concept “church fellowship,” which among some within the United Church have been set in circulation and threaten to gain accepted standing.

A couple of statements in recent times serve to cast a sharp light upon these notions, dangerous to the congregation. In an issue of “Kirkebladet” there was recently—indeed by a stealthy side way—smuggled in the remark that Augsburg was a “private” opposition school.

In “Samfundet” No. 2 it is said of Prof. Oftedal—and it must be applied also to Prof. Sverdrup—that he “until quite recently functioned as theological professor.”

Such a mode of expression—apart from the childish insult intended in it—rests upon the view that when Augsburg is no longer the seminary of the United Church, then it is a private institution, and that when Professors Sverdrup (and Oftedal) have resigned as theological professors in the United Church, then they altogether are no longer theological professors, however much they continue at Augsburg, as they now have soon done for 20 years and 17 years before anyone had yet heard mention of the United Church.

Such artless statements and conceptions spring again not only from that notion of the significance of the United Church, exaggerated beyond all reason, which the majority’s pastors have set in circulation, but essentially, and in the last instance, from an altogether Catholicizing and anti-congregational concept of church fellowship, which the pastors of the old or synodical tendency with all diligence cultivate and put into effect—when it can serve their advantage and strengthen their power over the congregation.

They regard the “fellowship” namely as the higher form of the congregation, and especially their own fellowship as the real manifestation of the one Church, make it identical with “the Church,” and regard everything outside it as sectarianism.

But is the fellowship such a most holy thing, that the congregation in it as it were disappears, and is given over into the hands of the fellowship?

No, by no means.

Not even the practical carrying out of this by deciding, at the command of a few men, upon the erection of a new and thereby necessarily oppositional seminary, without asking a single congregation for counsel, alters the matter in the least.

A church fellowship under our free conditions is nothing other than a free mutual agreement between a greater or lesser number of congregations—the number makes no difference to the matter—to work together in certain common undertakings, as for example a seminary, mission, publication of religious books, etc., where either the single congregation is unable to do it alone, or where the union of common forces will bring each congregation a proportionately greater benefit. Such a union of congregations for mutual support and help is therefore not any larger congregation or a higher form of the congregation; for the Church’s fundamental unit is and remains, according to God’s Word, the congregation alone, and no church fellowship, not even the Catholic Church, has, without vigorous contradiction from others, been able to arrogate to itself the right to be regarded as “the Church.”

Such a Catholicizing concept of a church fellowship therefore cannot be presupposed or practiced without the greatest damage to the congregation’s independence and Christian liberty, and will, since it has only the purpose of advancing clerical class interests and gradually bringing forth the episcopal office, unavoidably press the individual congregation and its spiritual need more and more aside, and let our Norwegian Lutheran Church gradually sink back into the Norwegian Synod’s state-churchliness and dead orthodoxy.

Since therefore the joining together of congregations in a fellowship is essentially an expedient arrangement for the joining together of spiritual forces and the saving of means, it is likewise not such a most holy institution, into which it is easy to come, but which a congregation cannot leave without making itself guilty of schism, sundering, and sin. If a congregation by free decision enters into a fellowship in the hope of finding there spiritual help and mutual edification, and it finds itself disappointed in this expectation, then it is its incontestable right, which no clergy under papal overreach can obstruct, to withdraw from such a fellowship-connection whenever it for itself finds it serviceable. And although every congregation both ought and will use caution in the exercise of this right and not in all things look only to its own advantage, but also to the advantage of its sister congregation, still it becomes a downright step toward Catholicism when a clergy, bound together by artificial means, in any way seeks to arrogate to itself authority to hinder a free Lutheran congregation in this inviolable right to join itself to whatever Lutheran fellowship-connection it finds good.

When now the majority in the United Church, through its leaders, lays claim to such extraordinary weightiness for its fellowship, that a seminary incorporated for nearly twenty-five years at once becomes a “private” opposition school as soon as the said leaders, against faith and honor, break their—moreover half-hearted—connection with it, or that its professors cease to be professors when they no longer desire to stand in connection with such leaders, then this is all the more absurd and ludicrous in this case, since the congregations of the United Church have not yet formed any completed fellowship-connection.

In 1890 it made the first essential step toward a full union by choosing common government and adopting a common seminary and common mission work for the congregations and fellowships participating in the work of union; but since other significant conditions were attached to this union, and these have not been fulfilled, and since one of the stipulations, namely concerning a “common seminary,” has even by the said leaders of the majority directly and without right been broken without consultation with the congregations, then it ought to be evident to everyone who has not become crazed by the majority that no union in the full sense of the word has yet come into being, and that it stands free for every congregation and every one of the three contracting parties, on account of the majority’s breach of contract, to draw back from the work of union at this stage without either causing division or withdrawing. For if it is the congregation’s indisputable right to withdraw even from older fellowships, when its welfare requires it, then obviously there can be no talk either of division or of withdrawal where not even any full fellowship-connection has been brought into being. In that case there can only be question of drawing back until a more satisfactory form of union might possibly become the subject of negotiation.

It is therefore wisely high time that the majority leaders tone down their lofty language about the extraordinary importance of that fellowship which the work of union aimed to bring about. The United Church is not identical with the United States, nor yet with the Church. It has one single and fully legitimate existence as a fellowship, and that not by virtue of its organization under the law of the congregation or of the Body of Christ, but simply by virtue of its incorporation under the civil law, and even that of a doubtful sort. It thus has the same significance as every other incorporated institution and thereby has—formally—received the right, of which it also has made ample use, namely to begin lawsuits. This right any three persons or three congregations whatsoever can acquire by being incorporated under the laws of the state, and is therefore not something over which to exalt oneself so exceedingly much.

A measure of sobriety in the concept of fellowship, and a modesty in the valuation of itself, one may demand even of old church fellowships and schools with a sufficiently fruitful past; the lack of these becomes then not only unchristian, but downright ludicrous in a fellowship which after two years of cooperation has still not reached any full union, or in a seminary which is not yet many days old.

[Sven Oftedal.]
