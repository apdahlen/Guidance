STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Tillag: appendix / supplement. In context, best rendered as “Appendix.”
* Optagelse i Menigheden: literally “admission into the congregation/church body.” “Reception into the congregation” or “admission to the congregation” both work, but the term must preserve ecclesial and constitutional force, not merely social belonging.
* Menighed: can mean congregation, church body, local church, or communion of believers depending on context. Here it often refers to the organized local congregation as a governing body, yet sometimes also carries theological resonance as the body of believers.
* Betingelser: conditions / qualifications / prerequisites. It has juridical and ecclesial force; not merely “requirements” in a casual sense.
* Bekjendelse: confession / profession. In this context, “profession” can fit idiomatically, but “confession” preserves doctrinal seriousness.
* Talemaader: ways of speaking / formulas of speech / modes of expression. “Stock phrases” may be too colloquial; the phrase warns against adopting pious language externally.
* sandt Liv i Gud: true life in God. This must retain spiritual and theological density, not be flattened into “real faith.”
* verdslig: worldly. This term is doctrinally charged and must not be diluted into merely “secular.”
* Skik paa Tingene: to bring order / proper shape to matters. Idiomatic; literal translation would sound awkward.
* Misbrug gaar i Svang: an abuse is in vogue / is current / is widespread. Best rendered idiomatically as “an abuse is prevalent” or “a corrupt practice is abroad.”
* Betryggelse ligeoverfor Evigheden: reassurance / security in relation to eternity. Must preserve the idea of false spiritual security before eternal judgment.
* Angreb paa Synder og Laster: attacks on sins and vices. “Vices” should remain, not be softened.
* Ryggesløsheder: recklessness / shameless disorders / moral irresponsibilities. Needs moral seriousness; “irresponsibility” may be too weak.
* nøie: carefully / strict / exact. In context, “very careful” or “very strict” about admission.
* Mand for Mand, Person for Person: man by man, person by person. Repetition is rhetorically important and should be preserved.
* Paaskrift som kristne: an inscription/endorsement as Christians; effectively a formal attestation or label. Must not be flattened into “approval.”
* Frelsen og Saligheden: salvation and blessedness. Classical theological pairing; should remain stable.
* Menighedsprotokollen: church register / congregational register. Not merely “membership list” if that loses formal tone.
* bevidst Tro: conscious faith. Important term; not simply “faith” or “personal faith,” since the emphasis is on conscious, self-aware profession.
* Menighedsraadet: congregational council / church council. Since this is constitutional context, “church council” or “congregational council” should sound institutional.
* Vidnesbyrd: testimony / witness. Here likely “testimony,” especially from those who have tested the person.
* prøvet Vedkommende: examined / tested / proved the person in question. Must preserve evaluative and ecclesial sense.
* værge sig mod: guard itself against / defend itself against. Organic, protective image.
* kjødelige Grunde: fleshly reasons / carnal motives. “Carnal” preserves theological register better than “worldly” here, since “verdslig” already covers worldly.
* Selvopholdelsens Drift: instinct of self-preservation. Organic metaphor; should be retained.
* fremmed Materie: foreign matter. Important metaphor from organism imagery; should stay vivid.
* ømtaaligste: most delicate / most sensitive / most finely susceptible. Needs to preserve reverent delicacy.
* Udskydelse af Menigheden: exclusion from the congregation. Must retain formal church-disciplinary sense.
* Embedsmænd: office-bearers / ministers / officers. In church context, “office-bearers” is likely best.
* erkjendte: recognized / acknowledged. Straightforward but formal.
* hellige Pligt: holy duty. Must remain strong.
* Frivillighedens Princip: the principle of voluntariness. Constitutional and ecclesiological term.
* Prøve paa Frivillighedens Art: a test of the character/nature of that voluntariness. Important logical phrase.
* Aandens Værk eller Kjødets: the work of the Spirit or of the flesh. Must preserve biblical antithesis.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long explanatory bracketed editorial introduction contains multiple subordinate clauses and historical framing; it must remain intelligible without collapsing into clipped modern prose.
* The speech itself uses brief, forceful aphoristic units followed by explanatory expansions. The contrast between short warnings and longer practical reasoning must be preserved.
* Repeated rhetorical questions: “Hvem skal optage? Hvordan er Menigheden?” must remain abrupt and probing.
* The sermon-like logic often accumulates through coordination rather than analytic subdivision; over-punctuation or excessive sentence splitting would flatten the cadence.
* Parallel constructions such as “Mand for Mand, Person for Person” and “dels ... dels ...” must remain visibly parallel.
* The 1905 paragraph is one long, developing argument with organic metaphor and theological conclusion; breaking it too much would weaken inevitability and oral force.
* Semicolon logic and comma-accumulation carry doctrinal reasoning forward. These need English equivalents that preserve progression.

Theological Hazards

* True life in God versus false speech about Jesus: distinction between verbal profession and spiritual reality must remain sharp.
* Worldly congregation / worldly pastor: doctrinal critique of visible church corruption; must not be softened into administrative dysfunction.
* Admission into the congregation is explicitly distinguished from certifying someone as Christian; this ecclesiological distinction is central.
* Membership does not confer salvation; salvation can occur apart from one’s name being entered in the register. This must remain doctrinally explicit.
* Baptism, salvation, and being added to the congregation echo New Testament order and must preserve scriptural force.
* Conscious faith as basis for participation in governance carries ecclesial and spiritual significance.
* The church as Christ’s body, the highest and most delicate of organisms, is a controlling metaphor with ecclesiological seriousness.
* The antithesis between Spirit and flesh governs the final sentence and must retain biblical weight.
* Church discipline in admission and exclusion is presented as a holy duty toward the Lord and His body; this cannot be diluted into policy language.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, practical, probing, and unsentimental. Any modernization toward “inclusive” or “pastoral-soft” language would erase Sverdrup’s severity.
* Warnings about false assurance, abuse, and manipulation of church membership must remain stark.
* The speaker uses direct moral pressure through simple assertions rather than emotional flourish. The translation must preserve this dry intensity.
* Constitutional and theological registers intermingle; the translation must not separate them too sharply.
* The oral cadence of ecclesial deliberation is important: the speech sounds spoken, but weighty.
* Repetition and plainness generate force. Stylistic embellishment would distort voice.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “Man kan ogsaa tale falskt om Jesus” does not mean merely to lie doctrinally; it means one may speak in a false way about Jesus, adopting pious language without truth of heart. The force is spiritual, not merely semantic.
* “lægge sig til visse forvovne Talemaader om Jesus” means to adopt bold, overconfident, perhaps presumptuous formulas about Jesus. “Forvovne” suggests daring, forward, over-bold speech; it warns against pietistic overstatement detached from reality.
* “sandt Liv i Gud” is a pietistic and biblical expression for genuine spiritual life rooted in God. A literal rendering is best because explanatory substitutes such as “authentic Christianity” would lose biblical resonance.
* “faa Skik paa Tingene” is idiomatic: to get matters into proper order. Literal rendering would sound mechanical.
* “En Misbrug gaar i Svang” is an idiom for a practice being current or widespread. The phrase denotes more than isolated misuse; it is something socially active and spreading.
* “Betryggelse ligeoverfor Evigheden” means a felt assurance over against eternity, especially judgment and the life to come. Theologically, it refers to a false security sought through institutional belonging.
* “Betryggelse mod Angreb paa Synder og Laster” means protection from moral rebuke. The implication is that some seek membership to shield their sins from ecclesial discipline.
* “drive Presten bort” shows practical church politics: people may be brought in to remove a pastor who rebukes sin. This must remain blunt.
* “optager Mand for Mand, Person for Person” emphasizes individual discernment rather than mass reception. The doubled formula intensifies care and particularity.
* “giver dem Paaskrift som kristne” means granting formal endorsement or labeling them as Christians. Literal “inscription” would obscure the force. The concern is ecclesial certification.
* “lagt til Menigheden” strongly echoes Acts 2:41, 47, where those saved are “added” to the church. This is a clear biblical idiom and should be rendered in a way that preserves the echo: “were added to the congregation/church.”
* “kommet til bevidst Tro” means having come to conscious faith, not merely having faith in some hidden or developmental sense. It implies aware, owned, professed faith.
* “Noget Vidnesbyrd bør der være” means some testimony ought to be present. It implies evidentiary attestation, not merely subjective impression.
* “har prøvet Vedkommende” means those who have tested or examined the person concerned. It carries ecclesial discernment rather than scientific testing.
* “værge sig mod den Fare” evokes guarding or defending the congregation against danger; this fits the organic metaphor that follows.
* “Alt som lever og voxter, har Selvopholdelsens Drift i sig” invokes a creation-wide principle of living organisms preserving themselves. The church is then analogically described as the supreme organism.
* “fremmed Materie” is likely drawn from biological or medical imagery: foreign matter threatening an organism. Literal translation is effective and vivid.
* “Frivillighedens Princip” concerns free, voluntary joining, but Sverdrup argues that examination does not violate voluntariness; it tests whether the voluntariness is spiritual or fleshly.
* Biblical allusions and natural English equivalents:
* The strongest natural equivalent from English Bible tradition is “added to the church/congregation” for “lagdes til Menigheden.”
* “the work of the Spirit or of the flesh” naturally echoes Pauline diction and should remain close to biblical phrasing.
* Literal translation would obscure force in phrases such as “faa Skik paa Tingene,” “En Misbrug gaar i Svang,” and “giver dem Paaskrift som kristne.” These require idiomatic English while preserving seriousness.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

First passage

* Thesis: Verbal statements about Jesus are not reliable signs of true spiritual life.
* Pivot to the real issue: the difficulty lies less in admission itself than in the condition of the congregation doing the admitting.
* Diagnostic questions: Who admits? What is the congregation like?
* Supporting claim: a worldly congregation and worldly pastor will naturally admit those like themselves.
* Contrastive claim: where believers and a believing pastor are present, admission difficulties diminish.
* Broader diagnosis: under present conditions in most places, true reform is practically impossible because abuse is already widespread.
* Specification of abuses:
* Some seek membership for reassurance regarding eternity.
* Others seek protection against rebuke of sins and vices within the congregation.
* Concrete example: people may be brought into membership in order to expel a pastor who rebukes moral disorder.
* Practical conclusion: therefore admission should be handled very carefully and individually.
* Clarification: admission here means granting participation in congregational government, not certifying Christian status or granting access to hear God’s Word.
* Reassurance: stricter admission does not hinder anyone from salvation and blessedness.
* Doctrinal support: one may be saved without one’s name being in the church register.
* Scriptural qualification: according to God’s Word, the baptized and saved were also added to the congregation.
* Constitutional inference: therefore a congregation likely has the right to say that only those come into governance who have come to conscious faith.
* Editorial/legal refinement: Sverdrup would prefer the constitutional point worded to align with VII, 4.
* Final procedural conclusion: there should be some testimony, preferably from those who have tested the applicant.

Second passage

* Opening principle: as congregations age, grow, and gain standing, many motives may arise for joining them.
* Necessity follows: the congregation must guard itself against admitting members drawn not by God’s Spirit but by fleshly motives.
* Analogy from life: all living, growing things possess self-preserving instinct and seek to exclude foreign matter.
* Heightened application: therefore the congregation, as Christ’s body and the highest, most delicate organism, must be careful both in admitting and excluding members.
* Means: through its office-bearers and congregational meetings, it exercised the control recognized as a holy duty toward the Lord and His body.
* Anticipated objection: this does not conflict with voluntariness.
* Resolution: it merely imposes a necessary and rightful test on the quality of that voluntariness, whether it is of the Spirit or of the flesh.

This structure must remain intact. The translation should preserve the movement from diagnostic observation to doctrinal distinction to practical ecclesial rule.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* “sandt Liv i Gud” echoes Johannine and Pauline life-in-God language; compare 1 John’s tests of life, abiding, and truth, though no direct quotation appears.
* “de, som blev døbt og frelste ogsaa lagdes til Menigheden” strongly echoes Acts 2:41 and Acts 2:47, where those received and saved are added to the church.
* “Guds Aand” and “kjødelige Grunde” anticipate Pauline Spirit/flesh antithesis; compare Galatians 5:16-24, Romans 8:1-13.
* “Kristi Legeme eller Menigheden” clearly echoes Pauline body imagery; compare 1 Corinthians 12, Ephesians 1:22-23, Colossians 1:18.
* “hans Legeme” reinforces the same Pauline ecclesiology.
* “Aandens Værk eller Kjødets” again sounds distinctly Pauline, especially Galatians and Romans.
* The distinction between outward confession and inward truth resonates with passages such as Matthew 7:21-23 and Titus 1:16, though the wording is not a direct quotation.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings about false speech concerning Jesus must remain severe and unsentimental.
* The diagnosis of worldly congregation and worldly pastor should sound like moral-spiritual indictment, not organizational criticism.
* “Jo strengere man er jo bedre” must retain its edge: “the stricter one is, the better.” Any softening would alter the pastoral logic.
* The statement that strictness hinders no one from salvation must sound categorical and calm, not defensive.
* The 1905 paragraph should build pressure by accumulation; the reader should feel the inevitability of churchly vigilance.
* The final contrast, Spirit or flesh, should land with decisive biblical force.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “maatte kræve” = “had the right to require” in the editorial framing; preserve authority.
* “maa sættes i Forbindelse med” = “must be connected with”; necessity, not suggestion.
* “vilde der ikke blive saa store Vanskeligheder” = “there would not be such great difficulties”; conditional but firm.
* “er det rent ud umuligt” = “it is simply impossible” or “flatly impossible”; strong impossibility.
* “Det er derfor rigtig at være ganske nøie” = “it is therefore right to be very careful/strict”; moral rightness, not mere prudence.
* “Jo strengere man er jo bedre” = comparative maxim that must retain uncompromising force.
* “har den vist Ret til det” = “it surely has the right to do so” or “it evidently has the right to do so”; confident ecclesial judgment.
* “bør der være” = “there ought to be”; retain obligation.
* “saa maatte den søge at værge sig” = “it had to seek to guard itself”; necessity arising from circumstances.
* “var omhyggelig” = “was careful” but in context stronger: “was careful indeed / was scrupulous.”
* “den erkjendte som sin hellige Pligt” = “which it recognized as its holy duty”; solemn obligation.
* “det stred jo ikke” = “this did not, of course, conflict”; argumentative force should remain.
* “det lagde jo kun en nødvendig og berettiget Prøve” = “it only imposed a necessary and justified test”; necessity and legitimacy both must stand.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Menighed = congregation, unless “church” is needed for natural biblical echo. Keep stable where possible.
* Optagelse i Menigheden = admission to / reception into the congregation.
* sandt Liv i Gud = true life in God.
* verdslig = worldly.
* frelst / Frelsen = saved / salvation.
* Saligheden = blessedness.
* bevidst Tro = conscious faith.
* Vidnesbyrd = testimony.
* Guds Aand = God’s Spirit.
* kjødelige Grunde = fleshly motives/reasons.
* Kristi Legeme = Christ’s body.
* Aanden / Kjødets = the Spirit / the flesh.
* hellige Pligt = holy duty.

Rendering choice for Menighed

* In the first passage, “congregation” works best for both institutional and local church sense.
* In the explicit Acts echo, “added to the congregation” preserves continuity with the local body under discussion while still sounding biblical.
* In the 1905 passage, “the congregation” remains preferable, though “church” could be justified in “Christ’s body or the congregation.” I will keep “congregation” for consistency.

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Membership used as false “reassurance over against eternity” must retain the image of seeking shelter from eternal realities.
* The congregation as living organism with instinct of self-preservation must remain fully metaphorical, not paraphrased into mere institutional caution.
* “foreign matter” must remain vivid and biological.
* “Christ’s body” must remain the governing ecclesial image and not be reduced to “community.”
* The Spirit/flesh contrast is itself metaphorically and biblically saturated; it must not be psychologized.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The first passage moves between warning, diagnosis, practical governance, and doctrinal reassurance.
* Severity appears in exposure of false profession, worldliness, abuse, and manipulation.
* Consolation appears in the statement that strict admission hinders no one from salvation and blessedness.
* Scriptural sobriety follows in the reminder that the saved were also added to the congregation.
* The second passage is more elevated and conceptual, moving from sociological observation to organic metaphor to ecclesial principle.
* These tonal shifts should remain distinct; severity should not swallow consolation, and reassurance should not weaken discipline.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve the short spoken blows: “Who is to admit? What is the congregation like?”
* Keep the measured repetitions: “man by man, person by person.”
* Allow longer accumulative sentences in the 1905 excerpt so the argument gathers weight aloud.
* Maintain semicolon pivots where they reproduce deliberative and sermonic rhythm.
* Avoid clipped modern prose. The text should sound speakable before an assembly.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Appendix.

On Admission to the Congregation.

[At the Free Church’s annual meeting in 1898, which among other things discussed a draft for a congregational constitution, there was discussion of what conditions a congregation had the right to require of those who applied for admission to the congregation. Some held that the congregation ought to require a confession, for example: “I believe in Jesus,” “I know that I love Jesus,” or the like. In the course of the conversation Sverdrup expressed himself as follows, according to the report in Foldebladet for June 22, 1898:]

“One may also speak falsely about Jesus. One may adopt certain over-bold ways of speaking about Jesus and mean little by them; so neither is that any sure sign of true life in God.

The difficulty is not admission to the congregation, but the difficulties must be placed in connection with the congregation itself. Who is to admit? What is the congregation like?

When the congregation is worldly and the pastor is worldly, then they naturally admit such as are like themselves. If, on the other hand, one had an assembly of believers and a believing pastor, there would not be such great difficulties with admission to the congregation.

As conditions are in most places, it is simply impossible to get matters into proper order. An abuse is abroad. Many enter the congregation in order to obtain reassurance over against eternity; others want protection against attacks upon sins and vices within the congregation. When, for example, a pastor rises up against acts of moral recklessness, then people are brought into the congregation in order to drive the pastor away.

It is therefore right to be very careful about admission to the congregation; above all, that one admit man by man, person by person. Admission in this connection means nothing other than that one gives those who are admitted occasion to be among those who govern the congregation, not that one gives them an endorsement as Christians or gives them access to hear God’s Word.

The stricter one is, the better. One thereby hinders no one from salvation and blessedness.

One can be saved even if one does not have one’s name in the congregational register.

According to God’s Word we see that those who were baptized and saved were also added to the congregation. If now some congregation wished to draw a boundary and say: only when you have come to conscious faith may you take part in governing the congregation, then it surely has the right to do so.

I should prefer to write the point differently, so that it might come into agreement with VII, 4, where it says that the congregational council shall ‘examine whether those who request admission to the congregation have the requisite conditions for it.’ There ought to be some testimony, and preferably the testimony of those who have tested the person concerned.”

---

[In accordance with what has been communicated above, Sverdrup at the Free Church’s 9th annual meeting, 1905, gave an address on the conditions for the formation of a congregation, in which he says:]

“It lies in the nature of the matter that, as the congregations grew older and increased in number and esteem, and there came to be many different reasons that might move a person to join the congregation, it had to seek to guard itself against the danger that lies in admitting such members as are drawn to the congregation not by God’s Spirit, but by fleshly reasons. Everything that lives and grows has within itself the instinct of self-preservation and seeks to keep away and to remove what is called ‘foreign matter.’ It is therefore also self-evident that the highest and most delicate of all organisms, Christ’s body, or the congregation, was careful both with admission into and exclusion from the congregation. And partly through its office-bearers and partly through the congregation’s meetings, the congregation exercised the oversight which it recognized as its holy duty toward the Lord and His body. But this did not, of course, conflict with the principle of voluntariness; it only imposed a necessary and justified test upon the character of that voluntariness, whether it was the work of the Spirit or of the flesh.”
