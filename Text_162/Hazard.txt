STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Prestesamfund: Not simply “clerical association,” but a church order in which the clergy constitute the effective ruling body. In this article it is polemical and must retain the sense of clergy-dominion, not mere professional fellowship.
* Menighedsfrihed: More than “congregational freedom” in a modern administrative sense; it implies the rightful spiritual liberty of the congregation under the Word of God, over against clerical domination.
* Menighedssamfund: A contested label. The author argues that some use this term disingenuously for what is in reality still a clergy-controlled body.
* Frikirke: Must be rendered with care. In context it is not merely a denominational label, but a claim to genuine ecclesial freedom; the author implies that the name can be used falsely.
* sømmeligt Vis: Not merely “properly,” but in a becoming, decent, honorable manner.
* Samfundsspørgsmaalet: Not a vague “social question,” but the question of church polity, communal ordering, and ecclesial constitution.
* „Kristi Legemes“ Skyld: Strong biblical-theological language. Must preserve the sense of “for the sake of the Body of Christ.”
* give Rum for: More than “allow”; it means make room for, afford proper scope to.
* uretmæssig ... Magt og Myndighed: Illegitimate power and authority, especially as exercised contrary to Scripture.
* statskirkelige Presteparti: Not merely a political party, but the clerical faction formed by state-church assumptions.
* fra Barnsben: “From childhood onward,” with emphasis on deep formation.
* gjennemtrukne af Fordomme: Thoroughly imbued, saturated, permeated with prejudices.
* enfoldige Sandhed: Here “simple” in the sense of plain, straightforward, unadorned; not “naive.”
* saakænt Arbeide: An ironic expression: a thankless, fruitless, or dreary labor.
* tendensiøse Bemerkninger: Remarks written with a partisan slant, not neutral observation.
* eier og bestyrer: Owns and administers/governs.
* falsk Lære / Kjætteri / Vranglærere: Must preserve confessional sharpness: false doctrine, heresy, false teachers.
* pretenderet guddommelig Authoritet: Claimed or pretended divine authority.
* Alderen[s] Hævdøret: The prescriptive right acquired by long custom or long-standing precedent.
* Formyag/Formynderskab sense in “som Formiag”: The text appears corrupt or archaic in print; the sense is that congregations are considered at most as wards, minors, or dependents, not rightful agents.
* hjælpeløse: Helpless, powerless; important because the article disputes where the true helplessness lies.
* Retfærdiggjørelse: Justification, in the full Lutheran doctrinal sense.
* Retsroenhed: The print seems unstable; likely intended to indicate rightness, soundness, orthodoxy, or doctrinal trustworthiness. The sentence clearly asks whether congregations are thereby secured regarding the doctrinal soundness of theological teachers.
* Naadevalgslære: The doctrine of election/grace-election; must retain its confessional specificity.
* Remedium: Remedy; keep the elevated polemical register.
* Procesing / Begjær efter uretmæssig Eiendom: Litigiousness or legal wrangling, and desire for property not rightfully one’s own.
* Skattesubjekt: A taxable subject/object; the word is biting and reductive.
* aandsløst Presteherredømme: Spiritless clerical rule; not merely authoritarian, but lifeless, spiritually deadening domination.
* purt og rent: A confessional formula meaning purely and rightly/purely and cleanly.
* agtes og oplefles: Likely “esteemed and built up/nurtured”; must preserve the sense of spiritual life being valued and fostered.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long periodic sentences carry polemical weight by accumulation; they should not be broken up unnecessarily.
* The prose relies on adversative pivots: “ikke ...; ikke heller ...; thi ...”; “Og ikke desto mindre ...”; “Men ...”; “Nuvel ...” These logical turns must remain explicit.
* Repeated rhetorical questions build pressure and implicate the reader; they must be preserved in sequence.
* Parenthetical clauses and inserted qualifications carry argumentative precision; they should not be flattened.
* Quotation marks are often polemical distancing devices, especially around “Kirkesamfund,” “Kirken,” and “Kristi Legeme.”
* Biblical citation is woven syntactically into the sentence rather than isolated; this integration should remain.
* The article uses historical analogy in extended comparison; the translator must preserve parallel structure between Reformation-era priests, the Norwegian state church, the Synod, and the United Church.

Theological Hazards

* The distinction between clergy-rule and the rightful ordering of Christ’s congregation is theological, not merely administrative.
* “Christ’s Body,” “congregation,” “Word of God,” “pure doctrine,” “spiritual life,” and “justification by personal faith” must remain doctrinally precise.
* The citation from 1 Peter 5:3 is central: the clergy are not to lord it over the congregations. The contrast between shepherding and ruling is implied.
* The article assumes that church order must be normed by Scripture no less than doctrine. This principle is essential and must not be weakened.
* Terms relating to spiritual life and death, purity of doctrine, heresy, and congregational rights carry confessional force.
* “Grace-election” controversy and charges of false teaching are presented from within a real confessional struggle; the translation must preserve the seriousness without editorial smoothing.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is severe, earnest, accusatory, and exhortational; modern neutral prose would erase its force.
* Irony appears throughout, especially where the author cites opponents’ language; it must remain controlled, not exaggerated.
* The author speaks not as detached historian but as ecclesial combatant addressing conscience and judgment.
* The rhythm is sermonic and editorial at once: morally weighted, cumulative, and unsparing.
* Repetition of “Men, siger du ...” and “Naar ...” creates oral pressure and should be maintained.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “Der er ingen døvere end den som ikke vil høre.”
  Meaning: No one is deafer than the one who will not hear. This is a proverbial moral statement about willful refusal, not inability.
  Implication: The problem is culpable resistance, not lack of evidence.
  English rendering: “There are none deafer than the one who will not hear.”

* “fra Barnsben”
  Meaning: From earliest childhood.
  Implication: Prejudice is not incidental but deeply formed.

* “gjennemtrukne af Fordomme”
  Meaning: Thoroughly saturated with prejudices.
  Implication: Their whole mode of perception has been conditioned.

* “lyder som et dem ganske fremmed og uforstaaeligt Sprog”
  Meaning: Sounds to them like a wholly foreign and incomprehensible language.
  Implication: Scriptural speech about the congregation has become unintelligible within a clericalized framework.

* “med Forbigaaelse af Fædrene blev begrundet i Guds Ord selv”
  Meaning: Was grounded in God’s Word itself, passing by the Fathers.
  Scriptural-theological implication: The formal principle of the Reformation—Scripture over inherited authority.

* “Kristi Legeme” / “Menigheden”
  Biblical context: 1 Corinthians 12; Ephesians 1, 4; Colossians 1.
  Implication: The congregation is not a human association but the living Body of Christ.
  Natural English equivalent: “the Body of Christ.”

* “ikke som de der vilde herske over Menighederne”
  Biblical source: 1 Peter 5:3.
  KJV offers “Neither as being lords over God’s heritage,” but the user’s instructions require modern pronouns and resistance to inherited KJV tone. A closer modern rendering in this prose context is “not as those who would rule over the congregations.”

* “Læres purt og rent”
  Confessional phrase meaning doctrine taught purely and without corruption.
  Literal rendering “pure and clean” would sound awkward; doctrinally faithful English is “taught purely and rightly” or “taught pure and undefiled.” In final translation, “purely and rightly” best preserves confessional sense without archaism.

* “Guds Liv agtes og oplefles”
  Pietistic expression referring to the life of God in the soul/congregation being esteemed and fostered.
  Literalism would obscure the spiritual force; a faithful rendering should preserve both reverence and nurture.

* “Splittelse, Splittelse”
  Repetition intensifies moral indictment.
  Meaning: Division was the only remedy they found.
  Must remain exactly emphatic.

* “som et Skattesubjekt”
  Meaning: As a source of revenue, an object to be taxed.
  Implication: The congregation is reduced to financial utility under clerical rule.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening thesis: It often seems almost hopeless to write on this subject, because clergy determined to preserve clergy-rule refuse to hear.
* First supporting claim: Their refusal arises not only from insincerity or self-interest, but from deep state-church conditioning that makes scriptural teaching on the congregation sound foreign.
* Historical analogy: This is compared to the priesthood in the age of the Reformers, who found justification by faith alone incomprehensible and heretical because it was grounded directly in Scripture rather than in the Fathers.
* Doctrinal principle: The ordering of the congregation and the relation of congregations to one another, since spiritual life depends so much on it, must be grounded and regulated by God’s Word just as doctrine must be.
* Practical reason for continuing the debate: The laity—men and women in the congregations—have begun to think seriously and independently about Christ’s Body.
* Occasioning example: A partisan note in Lutheraneren concerning Prof. Briggs and theological education is cited.
* Conceptual clarification: The author distinguishes “church body” language from “the congregations,” arguing that historically “church body” denotes structures in which clergy rule and congregational rights are ignored.
* Historical tests of the opponent’s principle:

  * Roman Catholic example: Clerical power was not helpless, yet it protected false judgment, not truth.
  * Norwegian state church example: Clerical control over doctrine and appointments did not prevent rationalism.
  * Norwegian Synod example: Ownership and control of a seminary do not secure doctrinal truth for congregations if clergy control the vote.
* Central rhetorical question: If clergy possess decisive control and their doctrinal position is false, what remedy remains for the congregations?
* Historical answer: In the Synod controversy, the only remedy found was division.
* Extension of indictment: Clerical systems have brought bitterness, factionalism, litigation, and wrongful desire for property.
* Application to the United Church: Even constitutional hopes of congregational rights fail when clergy gain the upper hand.
* General conclusion: Remove freedom and voluntariness from the congregations and replace them with clergy-rule, and the end will be absolute and spiritless clerical dominion.
* Final counterpoint and conclusion: Presbyterians and free congregations possess a remedy—they may withdraw support from schools that fail to preserve congregational rights and spiritual life. True freedom allows support to go where God’s Word is taught purely and spiritual life is honored and nurtured.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* “Kristi Legeme” — clear echo of Pauline ecclesiology, especially 1 Corinthians 12:27; Ephesians 1:22–23; 4:12, 16; Colossians 1:18.
* “ikke som de der vilde herske over Menighederne” — explicit citation/adaptation of 1 Peter 5:3.
* “den enkeltes Retfærdiggjørelse alene ved sin egen personlige Tro” — strong Reformation shorthand rooted in Romans 3–5 and Galatians 2–3.
* “Guds Ord” as supreme ground over against “Fædrene” — a Reformation scriptural principle with broad resonance rather than a single proof-text.
* “Guds Menighed” — biblical language for the congregation/church, with resonance from Acts 20:28; 1 Corinthians 1:2; 10:32; 11:22; 15:9.
* “Guds Ord læres purt og rent” — confessional phrasing echoing Lutheran doctrinal formulas about the pure teaching of the Word.
* “Guds Liv” — not a fixed quotation, but likely resonant with Johannine and Pauline life language, as well as pietistic discourse about divine life in believers.
* The opposition between outward power and spiritual right resonates with broad biblical critique of domineering religious authority.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The translation must preserve the author’s severe impatience with clerical evasiveness.
* Charges such as lack of sincerity, prejudice, self-interest, domination, vengeance, factionalism, and wrongful property desire must not be softened.
* Repeated rhetorical questioning must retain its prosecuting force.
* Ironical quotation marks around opponents’ terms must remain where needed to signal critique.
* The conclusion must sound like a warning drawn from observed inevitability, not a speculative opinion.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “vil have” must retain determination: “will have,” not merely “prefer.”
* “maatte forsøges” and “maatte opgive” carry necessity: “something would have to be attempted,” “they would have to relinquish.”
* “skulde dog være” carries moral-logical necessity: “ought surely to be” or “should surely be.”
* “maa begrundes og reguleres” must remain strong: “must be grounded and regulated.”
* “kan ikke engang siges” must remain categorical: “cannot even be said.”
* “kan ... tilbageholde” and “kan ... lægge sine Evner” signal real capacity and freedom, not weak possibility.

* Prestesamfund = clergy-body or clergy-dominion depending on context; in final translation predominantly “clergy-body” where the institutional form is named, and “clerical rule” where the meaning is explicative.
* Menighed = congregation.
* Menighederne = the congregations.
* Menighedsfrihed = congregational freedom.

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* “give Rum for en fri og levende Menighed” must remain spatial and organic: make room for a free and living congregation.
* “gjennemtrukne af Fordomme” retains the image of being permeated through.
* “Presteskabs-lænker” must remain “chains of the clergy” or “clerical chains,” preserving bondage imagery.
* “Guds Liv” must remain vital and organic, not abstracted into “religious vitality” unless context absolutely requires it.
* “udstøte af Samfundet” carries bodily/social expulsion and should remain “cast out” or “expel.”

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The essay moves between denunciation, historical argument, sarcasm, sober warning, and constructive conclusion.
* The Roman Catholic and state-church examples are severe and polemical.
* The discussion of men and women in the congregations introduces a note of hope and seriousness.
* The conclusion shifts from indictment to remedy: withdrawal of support and free alignment with pure teaching and spiritual life.
* These tonal shifts must remain distinct.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve extended clause chains where the argument gains weight through cumulative syntax.
* Maintain the recurring “But, you say ...” oral challenge form.
* Preserve semicolon pivots and dash interruptions where they intensify the movement.
* Keep repetitions such as “Division, division” and “many, many centuries” in their oral-emphatic force.
* The close should sound fit for public reading: measured, grave, and final.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Clergy-Body or Congregational Freedom.
[Editorial article in “Folkeladet” for May 18, 1898.]

Many a time it seems almost hopeless to write on this subject, though for our Church it is so highly important. There are none deafer than the one who will not hear, and it seems futile to argue this matter with those pastors—of all Norwegian church bodies—who at any cost will have a clergy-body, even if they are willing to call it a congregational body or even a free church. It is not merely lack of sincerity that makes them so altogether unwilling to discuss the question of church order in a becoming manner; nor is it only the consciousness that something would have to be attempted for the sake of “the Body of Christ,” even if, in order to make room for a free and living congregation, they should have to relinquish an illegitimate power and authority, equally ruinous to both parties, according to the Word of God; “not as those who would rule over the congregations” (1 Pet. 5:3); for there is also an excuse for the state-church clerical party: from childhood onward, and by a state-church upbringing, they have been so altogether permeated with prejudices over against the congregational question, that even the simplest statements grounded in God’s Word concerning it sound like to them a wholly foreign and incomprehensible language, a language for which many of them lack the spiritual prerequisites to understand, even if they were willing.

It is with them as it was with the priesthood in the time of the Reformers; they were so thoroughly worked through with notions of “the extraordinary holiness of the office and its unlimited mediating authority,” so terrifyingly secure because their views had the sanction of many, many centuries and the testimony of the fathers on their side, that the Reformers’ word concerning the individual’s justification solely by his own personal faith sounded to them like a foreign and heretical speech, a speech sprung from self-interest, lust for domination, and ignobility, however much it was grounded—indeed precisely because, passing by the fathers, it was grounded in God’s Word itself.

It ought surely to be a simple and indisputable truth that the ordering of the congregation and the mutual connection of the congregations, upon which their spiritual life and activity so greatly depend, must be grounded and regulated by God’s Word just as much as doctrine. And nevertheless it seems almost impossible to get the state-church clerical party of The United Church to understand or acknowledge this plain truth, and on that basis to ground the discussion apart from all personal and foreign considerations.

And if it were not because men and women round about in the congregations in all the bodies had begun to think seriously and independently about this most holy thing among us, the Body of Christ, the congregation, it would be a thankless labor to convince narrow-sighted, prejudice-bound pastors of former days.

In “Lutheraneren” for May 11 there stand, on the occasion of Prof. Briggs, who, although judged by the Presbytery to be a heretic, is nevertheless to continue as theological teacher at Union Seminary, the following tendentious remarks:

“This shows how helpless a church body can become over against false doctrine, when it does not itself own and administer its seminary. It may declare a theological professor to be a heretic, and yet he continues all the same to spread his heresy within the body. It is precisely this relation that the leading Augsburgers have wished to place Augsburg Seminary in toward The United Church, and now most recently toward the Free Church.”

For the present we shall call attention to the fact that in the remark cited above—consistently with the language usage of The United Church—the expression “church body” is used, not “the congregations.” “Church body” is a word or concept that is not found in the New Testament; but in its historical development it has always denoted a communal connection in which the pastors have been either sole rulers or nearly sole rulers, partly on the basis of a pretended divine authority, as in the Catholic Church and the state churches, partly on the basis of a gradually heightened supremacy with the prescriptive right of age, as in most church bodies outside the state churches. The congregations and their right have in all these cases—except at the very most as wards—been left out of consideration.

Let us now make some observations drawn from historical reality concerning these “church bodies” and their “helplessness” over against heretical teachers.

When the Catholic church body, or “the Church,” as they were pleased to call it, deposed and condemned, and in part burned, such teachers as Wycliffe, Hus, and Jerome of Prague as heretics and false teachers, then surely on account of its power it was anything but “helpless.” But were the men named above therefore, from a Protestant point of view, heretics, or was there in the unrestricted power of the priesthood any guarantee for the congregations or for pure doctrine? Was not Luther entirely in his right when, on the ground of God’s Word and on behalf of God’s congregation, he protested against those teachers of theology whom the Catholic Church, with all authority and unanimity, appointed in all schools, and in whose appointment the people, who paid, had neither access nor right.

But, you say, they were Catholics and Jesuits, and we all know that they were heretical and jesuitical.

Let us then pass over to a church body with which we are well acquainted, the Norwegian Lutheran State Church. There too the priesthood had everything to say in matters of doctrine or theology, and the congregations were till the very latest unable to exercise any influence upon the choice of their own pastors or teachers.

In Hauge’s time the entire priesthood in Norway, together with the civil government, was rationalistic, and the teachers at the schools of higher learning were of the same sort. The state-church body, that is to say, the priesthood, had all the power; they were anything but “helpless,” and no one could prevent them from appointing rationalistic teachers of pastors. Is that the condition which the defenders of clergy-bodies regard as the one most in accord with God’s Word?

But, people say, that was a state church; here in this country we have free-church bodies. Very well, let us consider one of them, for example the Norwegian Synod. It makes claim to be free-church enough and asserts that it represents the congregations, in that they have the right to send representatives to the annual meetings. As is well known, the Norwegian Synod owns and controls its pastoral seminary as far as that is at all possible; otherwise the Anti-Missourians would doubtless have taken it too. Thus it is not helpless in this question. Are the congregations thereby now secured with respect to the doctrinal soundness of the theological teachers? [—] will be able to answer that better than Pastor Kildahl in Chicago.

For it is an equally well-known fact that, however much one may speak of free-church constitution and the right of the congregations in the Norwegian Synod, it is still the pastors who, partly through their pastors’ conferences, partly through the annual meetings, have the decisive, not to say the absolute, voice in all questions of the body, especially concerning doctrine and theological teachers.

If now the priesthood in the Norwegian Synod has the decisive voice in the choice of theological teachers, will it not require of them that they have the doctrinal position of the priesthood, or even of a majority of the priesthood? If now this position should happen to be false in doctrine, what remedy then have the congregations? Who then become helpless, the body or the congregations?

Is not this precisely what has happened in the Norwegian Synod, at least according to the Anti-Missourians’ understanding and course of action.

It helped nothing that the body owned and controlled the chair; it helped nothing that a number of congregations and pastors protested against the false doctrine of grace-election, and over it carried on the bitterest separations in earnest—the false teachers remained standing, upheld by the majority of the body, that is to say the majority of the pastors.

And what was the only remedy that the opposing part of the body, namely the Anti-Missourians, could find? Division, division.

The anti-Missourian pastors split the body; indeed, more than that, they went around from congregation to congregation, split as many as they could, brought bitterness and party-spirit into hearts, tore up the Norwegian church people anew under the leadership of a German professor, and led them into litigious wrangling and desire for property not rightfully theirs, which has followed the Anti-Missourians down to their latest Augsburg lawsuit.

The facts lie before everyone’s eyes, and each may draw his own conclusions. In truth, the clergy-bodies have been neither a spiritual nor a temporal blessing for our Norwegian church people. When the priesthood with its leaders has gained so complete an upper hand in The United Church, where by the constitution one had nevertheless hoped to secure to the congregations some right, that they have even been able to cast out of the body congregations and lawfully chosen delegates partly in order to satisfy personal revenge, partly because the congregations had maintained a school which The United Church now in court claims that the expelled members of the congregation have always lawfully been corporate members of—what security then have the congregations in such a clergy-body, either for the doctrine of a seminary or for its property?

When that same church body has in addition incorporated itself, and in many ways gathered considerable property, so that the priesthood can gradually feel itself fairly secure in its power, what regard will one then have for the congregations except as a taxable subject?

. . . Oh no, take freedom and voluntariness away from the congregations, and put a priesthood with clerical rule in their stead—however well it may begin, it will soon end in an absolute and spiritless clerical dominion. And of The United Church it cannot even be said that it has the advantage of beginning well.

The Presbyterians have a remedy against Prof. Briggs’s rationalism; they can withdraw their support from his school.

And a free congregation has this remedy against a pastoral seminary which does not uphold the congregation’s right and does not labor for the preservation of spiritual life: it can withhold its support. And if it is free and unhindered by all bonds of church-body organization and chains of the clergy, it can, without any regard to such things, lay its powers there where God’s Word is taught purely and rightly, and the life of God is esteemed and nurtured. . . . .

[S. Oftedal.]
