STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

* Lexical Hazards

* Menigheden: not merely “church” in an institutional sense, but “the congregation” or “the fellowship/church” as a spiritually defined body; context must determine whether the visible organized congregation or the true congregation of believers is intended.

* ydre Organisation / udvortes Forbindelse / udvortes Tegn: “outward organization,” “external connection,” “outward signs.” These terms are crucial to the visible/invisible church distinction and must not be blurred.

* troende Mennesker / sande troende / virkelige Menighedslemmer: doctrinally weighty distinctions between nominal and true believers; must retain precision.

* Hyklere / hykkelske Mennesker: “hypocrites,” not merely “insincere people.” The moral and spiritual charge is strong.

* gjenstridige Syndere: “obstinate sinners” or “stubborn sinners,” with ecclesial-disciplining force.

* oplyses: here not “becomes enlightened” in a vague modern sense, but “is instructed / is made to know” by the Word.

* salig: more than “happy”; “saved and blessed” or “attain salvation/blessedness.”

* i Aand og Sandhed: biblical phrase, “in Spirit and truth” / “in spirit and truth”; must preserve scriptural resonance.

* begyndte i Aanden, fuldender i Kjødet: clear Pauline echo; must preserve theological contrast of Spirit/flesh.

* Hjertets hemmelige Skjul: “the heart’s secret hiddenness” or similar; inward spiritual state concealed from human judgment.

* Merker: “marks,” not merely “features.”

* Kjendsgjerning: “fact” or “actuality,” often with evidentiary weight; repeated term should be rendered consistently.

* de omtalte “sju Lignelser”: “the seven parables”; internal textual reference likely known to the author’s larger argument.

* Garn: “dragnet” rather than merely “net,” if preserving biblical specificity.

* Kar af Guld og Sølv ... af Træ og Ler ... til Ære ... til Vanære: must preserve vessel imagery and biblical diction.

* Belærelse: “instruction” or “edification by teaching”; older pedagogical/religious term.

* Forfærdelse: “dismay,” “terror,” or “horror”; stronger than mere surprise.

* Bodsprædiken: “preaching of repentance,” not generic moral preaching.

* udsondrende: “separating,” “setting apart”; has ecclesial and purgative force.

* bekjendte sine Synder: “confessed their sins”; biblical cadence should be preserved.

* bie-de: archaic spelling of “waited”; sense is deliberate divine restraint.

* kjødelige og verdslige Tanker: “carnal and worldly thoughts/notions,” not merely “materialistic ideas.”

* den lille Flok: likely biblical “little flock.”

* uværdige: “unworthy,” not only socially unsuitable.

* uredelig / redelig Syndserkjendelse: “dishonest / honest acknowledgment of sin”; moral-spiritual integrity is central.

* kalde sligt for Menighed: polemically refuses the term “congregation/church” for a coercive folk-church structure.

* Folkekirkens Magt: “the power of the established/national church”; context-sensitive; should preserve institutional coercion.

* smaat bevendt med: idiomatic; “there is pitifully little doing of it” / “it is miserably scant with doing the same.”

* uden Gunst og uden Frygt: idiomatic rhetorical pair; “without favor and without fear.”

* Trivsel / vantrives: “thrive / fail to thrive,” organic-life metaphor.

* Sønderknuselse: “brokenness” or “contrition,” but with spiritual crushing force.

* Forhærdelse: “hardening,” doctrinally significant.

* forsumpes / fordærves: “sinks into a swamp / becomes corrupted”; spiritual decay imagery must remain forceful.

* Redskaber: “instruments” in God’s hand, not mere “tools” in flat prose.

* Tjenere og Tjenerinder: “servants and handmaids” or “servants, men and women”; avoid flattening the ecclesial labor imagery.

* Kjød og Blod: “flesh and blood,” biblical idiom.

* levende sterke: likely “living strong” / “strong with life”; preserve living-force emphasis.

* Verdsliggjørelse: “worldliness” or “becoming worldly/worldly corruption.”

* Vrængebillede: “caricature,” “hideous distortion,” “travesty”; strong image of grotesque falsification.

* Jesu Brud: “Jesus’ bride” or “the Bride of Jesus/Christ”; ecclesial image must remain vivid.

* Syntactic Hazards

* Long accumulative sentences carry sermonic logic and should not be excessively broken up.

* Frequent semicolon pivots are argumentative, not ornamental; they often mark intensification or qualification.

* Repeated clause pairs (“baade ... og,” “ikke alene ... men ogsaa”) structure reasoning and contrast; must remain visible.

* Embedded scriptural citations and allusions are woven seamlessly into prose and should not be isolated or paraphrased away.

* Rhetorical questions function as moral indictment; they must remain sharp and direct.

* Parenthetical clarifications (“d. v. s.”) and concessive turns help preserve doctrinal exactness.

* Parallel imperatives and repeated nouns (“Lys, Lys og atter Lys”) must retain cadence and cumulative force.

* Theological Hazards

* The distinction between the true congregation and its visible outward organization is central; mistranslation could collapse invisible/visible church ecclesiology.

* True faith as necessary for salvation is stated plainly and non-negotiably.

* Outward membership without inward faith is presented as spiritually dangerous false comfort.

* Church discipline is treated as a holy calling of the congregation, including admonition and exclusion.

* Repentance, confession of sin, and honest self-acknowledgment are conditions of rightful entrance.

* Word-preaching is the primary divine means by which sleepers awaken, doubters are strengthened, and hypocrites are judged.

* Christ-preaching is presented as the only true basis of the congregation’s life; substituting anything else corrupts it.

* Cross-bearing and labor are not optional extras but essential means of preserving the congregation from worldliness.

* The relation between Gospel and cross is reciprocal and paradoxical: “the Gospel of the cross and the cross of the Gospel.”

* Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, pastoral, polemical, and exhortational; smoothing it into neutral academic prose would distort the author.

* The author often moves from exposition to warning to rebuke to appeal; those shifts must remain palpable.

* Sharp condemnations of hypocrisy, coercive church structures, and complacent clergy must retain their edge.

* The prose addresses conscience as much as intellect; moral urgency must be preserved.

* Repetition is part of the sermonic force and should not be stylistically trimmed.

* Elevated but plain doctrinal seriousness is preferable to either colloquial modernity or faux-archaic English.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “begyndte i Aanden, fuldender i Kjødet” echoes Galatians 3:3. Meaning: some begin in the Spirit yet end in the flesh. It names spiritual decline from grace into carnality. A close rendering should preserve the Pauline opposition between Spirit and flesh.
* “i Aand og Sandhed” echoes John 4:23–24. It marks genuine belonging before God, not merely outward attachment. English should preserve the scriptural phrase rather than paraphrase it.
* “Kar af Guld og Sølv ... af Træ og Ler ... til Ære, andre til Vanære” echoes 2 Timothy 2:20. The image distinguishes kinds of vessels within a great house and is applied here to the externally organized congregations. Biblical vessel diction should remain intact.
* “berede Herren et velskikket Folk” echoes Luke 1:17. It refers to John the Baptist preparing a people fit/prepared for the Lord. “A people prepared for the Lord” is a natural English equivalent.
* “Omvendelsens Daab” corresponds to the Synoptic and Acts phrasing of the baptism of repentance. This is doctrinally stronger than a vague “conversion baptism.”
* “som bekjendte sine Synder” echoes the Gospel accounts of those baptized by John confessing their sins (e.g., Matthew 3:6; Mark 1:5). A natural English equivalent is “who confessed their sins.”
* “den lille Flok” likely echoes Luke 12:32 (“little flock”). The phrase carries pastoral tenderness but here also marks the severe reduction of the visible following to a true remnant.
* “uden Gunst og uden Frygt” is an idiomatic rhetorical pairing: without favor and without fear. It means impartial, fearless proclamation.
* “Lys, Lys og atter Lys” is not merely metaphorical illumination but a biblical and spiritual contrast between divine truth and darkness. Literal preservation is best.
* “Sønderknuselse” carries the sense of contrition or being inwardly broken under God’s truth; “brokenness” may work, but the surrounding cadence must preserve its severity.
* “Kjød og Blod” is a biblical anthropological idiom for natural human weakness and aversion. “Flesh and blood” is the natural equivalent.
* “tage op sit Kors og bære sine Byrder” fuses biblical discipleship language of cross-bearing with burden-bearing. Literal preservation best retains the spiritual force.
* “Kristi Legeme” and “Jesu Brud” are ecclesiological metaphors rooted in Pauline and Johannine/apocalyptic biblical imagery. They must remain metaphorical, not explanatory.
* King James-like equivalents may sometimes arise naturally from familiar biblical English (“flesh and blood,” “little flock,” “confessed their sins,” “hardening”), but the final prose should avoid artificial archaism such as “thee” or “thou.”
* Expressions where literal translation would obscure force:

  * “det er saa forfærdelig smaat bevendt med at gjøre det efter dem” cannot be rendered woodenly; sense is that there is a miserably small amount of actually following their example.
  * “forsumpes” should not become a vague “deteriorates”; it suggests bogging down, stagnating, turning swamp-like.
  * “Vrængebillede” should not be reduced to “poor image”; it implies grotesque distortion, a shameful caricature.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis: Because not all members of the congregation’s outward organization are true believers, and because hypocrites often take false comfort from outward connection to the congregation, the congregation has a holy calling to cleanse itself by awakening proclamation, earnest admonition, and exclusion of open and obstinate sinners.
* Initial doctrinal clarification: In reality, the congregation consists of believers; only true believers are actual members.
* Qualification regarding visible order: In outward organization, people can and do remain who do not belong inwardly in spirit and truth; some also fall away inwardly while retaining outward connection.
* Explanation: This happens because the visible congregation must judge by outward marks and cannot judge the hidden state of the heart.
* Scriptural grounding: The author points less to the parables than to Acts, the Epistles, and Revelation as clearer evidence that organized congregations include mixed spiritual conditions.
* Practical implication: Because congregations include varying spiritual states, exhortation, warning, comfort, teaching, and encouragement must operate within them.
* Intensification by astonishment: It is alarming that this was already true in the earliest congregations, despite God’s special preparation of a believing people through John, Jesus, and the sending of the Spirit.
* Historical-redemptive illustration: John prepared a repentant people; Jesus did not organize the congregation amid mass enthusiasm but only after the cross had sifted out worldly expectations; then He formed the little flock by giving the Spirit.
* Polemical inference: Those who dismiss such strictness as exaggeration are opposing the pattern set by John, Jesus, and the Spirit of truth.
* Human depravity highlighted: Even so, hypocrites pressed into the outward organization, revealing both the power of divine truth and the falsity of human hearts.
* Present application: It is unsurprising that modern so-called congregations contain many false members; what is horrifying is that some ministers teach this to be right.
* Diagnosis of the established-church problem: Coercive inclusion by law, ban, and folk-church power has predictably produced corruption, especially because there is no real effort to recover the true concept and essence of the congregation.
* Turning-point question: What then is to be done? Not complacent comparison with Sodom, but the same course as John, Jesus, and the apostles.
* Primary remedy: Preach the Word fully, truthfully, personally, directly, without favor and without fear. “Light” in the church is the chief matter.
* Christological center: Truth is Jesus Christ; only true Christ-preaching builds the congregation, while substitutes corrupt and ruin it.
* Ongoing conflict: This is the fight for life in the congregation and must continue as long as the congregation is on earth.
* Secondary remedy linked to the first: Work. The congregation exists to labor; it is a company of workers, not spectators who hire a minister to act for them.
* Analogy: Like a choir that needs a director yet requires all to sing, the congregation needs leadership yet all must participate in service.
* Effect of labor: Shared spiritual labor helps preserve the congregation from worldliness.
* Intensified prescription: Let the work become heavy to flesh and blood, a real cross in Jesus’ footsteps; that very burden helps the congregation remain truly a congregation.
* Concluding warning and summary: If the congregation does not resist its worldliness by these means, it will become a disgrace. Since hypocrites can enter outward organization, the congregation must remain watchful and employ the Lord’s means. Only the Gospel of the cross and the cross of the Gospel can preserve it from becoming a shameful distortion of Christ’s body and Jesus’ bride.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* “i Aand og Sandhed” — John 4:23–24.
* “begyndte i Aanden, fuldender i Kjødet” — Galatians 3:3.
* “sju Lignelser” — likely Matthew 13 and related kingdom parables, though the author nuances their relevance.
* dragnet with good and bad fish — Matthew 13:47–48.
* workers in the vineyard — Matthew 20:1–16.
* ten virgins — Matthew 25:1–13.
* “Kar af Guld og Sølv ... af Træ og Ler ... til Ære, andre til Vanære” — 2 Timothy 2:20.
* John the Baptist preparing a people for the Lord — Luke 1:17.
* baptism of repentance — Matthew 3:11; Mark 1:4; Luke 3:3; Acts 13:24; 19:4.
* “confessed their sins” — Matthew 3:6; Mark 1:5.
* people wanting to make Jesus king — John 6:15.
* “the little flock” — Luke 12:32.
* “It shall be more tolerable for Sodom and Gomorrah in the day of judgment than for you” — Matthew 10:15; cf. 11:24.
* “without favor and without fear” is not a direct quotation but resonates with biblical fearlessness and impartiality in proclamation and judgment.
* light/darkness imagery throughout — broadly Johannine and Pauline.
* truth is Jesus Christ — cf. John 14:6 and Johannine truth theology.
* rich in mercy — Ephesians 2:4.
* servants/handmaids of God — biblical service language; compare Luke 1:38, Acts, Pauline servant language.
* take up the cross — Synoptic discipleship texts, e.g., Matthew 16:24; Mark 8:34; Luke 9:23.
* Christ’s body — Pauline ecclesiology, e.g., 1 Corinthians 12; Ephesians 1, 4.
* bride imagery — Ephesians 5:25–32; Revelation 19:7; 21:2, 9.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* Warnings against hypocrisy must remain severe, not softened into sociological observation.
* Rebukes of clergy who teach that mixed, worldly membership is proper must remain pointed.
* The author’s astonishment and horror are part of the argument’s force and should remain explicit.
* Appeals to action (“what is to be done?”) must sound urgent and necessary, not merely advisory.
* The final warning about the congregation becoming a disgrace and a grotesque distortion of Christ’s body must retain full gravity.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* maa: generally “must,” not “should,” unless idiomatically impossible.
* kan og vil hænde: “can and will happen,” preserving inevitability.
* skulde: often “should” in historical or intended sense, but context sometimes requires “was to” or “ought to.”
* vil modvirke: “will resist/counteract” in volitional sense.
* blir / skal blive: often “becomes / shall become / will become”; care needed to preserve prophetic certainty.
* kan ikke dømme: “cannot judge,” not “is not in a position to judge,” unless stylistically necessary.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Menigheden: usually “the congregation”; where broader ecclesial resonance is needed, “the church” may be considered, but consistency favors “the congregation” in this passage.
* ydre Organisation / udvortes Organisation: “outward organization.”
* troende / sande troende: “believers / true believers.”
* Menighedslemmer: “members of the congregation.”
* Hyklere / hykkelske: “hypocrites / hypocritical.”
* Guds Ord: “the Word of God.”
* Omvendelse / Bod: “repentance.”
* Sandhed: “truth.”
* Aand / Kjødet: “Spirit / flesh” where biblically charged.
* Forkyndelse: “proclamation” or “preaching”; “proclamation” often fits broader statements.
* Formaning: “admonition.”
* Paamindelse: “reminder” or “earnest reminder”; context may require “warning reminder.”
* Udelukkelse: “exclusion.”
* Verdslighed / Verdsliggjørelse: “worldliness / becoming worldly” or “worldly corruption.”
* Kristi Legeme: “Christ’s body.”
* Jesu Brud: “Jesus’ bride.”

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Net/dragnet, vessels, light, sleep/waking, wavering/falling, nourishment, cutting judgment, fire rekindled, choir, cross-bearing, body, bride, caricature/distortion: all must remain images, not be dissolved into abstractions.
* Organic metaphors of thriving and withering are central to the passage’s sense of congregational life.
* “forsumpes” especially requires preserving the swamp/bog image of corruption and stagnation.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The passage shifts between doctrinal explanation, scriptural reasoning, astonishment, horror, rebuke, exhortation, encouragement, and warning.
* Consolatory notes about God’s mercy and His raising instruments to rekindle the fire must remain distinct from the surrounding severity.
* The contrast between life and death, light and darkness, sincerity and hypocrisy, labor and sloth, true congregation and distorted counterfeit must remain sharp.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Preserve sentence length where the author builds momentum through accumulation.
* Keep rhetorical questions as spoken challenges to the conscience.
* Preserve repeated terms and triadic emphases (“Lys, Lys og atter Lys”).
* Maintain enough syntactic breadth for the text still to sound proclaimable rather than reduced to clipped exposition.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

4. Since not all members of the congregation’s outward organization are always believing people, and since such hypocrites often take false comfort from their outward connection with the congregation, it is the congregation’s holy calling to cleanse itself both by awakening proclamation of the Word of God, by earnest admonition and reminder, and by exclusion of open and obstinate sinners.

The congregation consists in reality of believing people. And just as every human being is instructed by the Word of God that he cannot be saved and blessed except through faith in Jesus, so all are likewise instructed by the Word of God that only when they are true believers are they also actual members of the congregation.

When, however, there is question of the congregation’s outward organization, then the Word of God and Christian experience down through church history bear witness that it both can and will happen that such persons are included in the organization as do not in spirit and truth belong to the congregation. Likewise, that it can and will happen that some, who began in the Spirit, end in the flesh, or fall away from the faith in their hearts, even though they retain the outward connection with the congregation’s organization. For these things take place in the heart’s secret hiddenness, and there are no definite outward signs of them.

This fact has its simple explanation in this, that the congregation’s outward organization must take account of the outward signs and marks of the congregation or of Christian life, and cannot judge the hidden state of the heart.

We say that the Word of God bears witness to this fact. And then we think not so much of the seven parables mentioned, as of the Acts of the Apostles, the Epistles, and the Revelation of John.

For of Jesus’ parables one may indeed rightly say that they speak rather of the effect of the Word in general than specifically of the congregation’s organization. For example, when the kingdom is compared with a dragnet that catches good fish and rotten fish, then the net is rather the Gospel in its work upon human souls than it is the congregation’s outward organization. Yet it is also right to say that at least several of the parables can be applied to the congregation’s organization, as for example the workers in the vineyard and the ten virgins.

But when we read the Acts of the Apostles and the Epistles and the Revelation, then we receive a full and clear light upon this matter, that the organized congregations, according to their outward boundaries, comprise not only vessels of gold and silver, but also of wood and clay, and some for honor, others for dishonor. It is also clearly shown that the New Testament contains the truth as it is applied to the most various spiritual conditions, and it is presupposed that these are found within the organized congregations.

In other words, just as each individual Christian is never all that he ought to be, so it is also with the congregation, and admonition, warning, encouragement, comfort and consolation, instruction and teaching must be used within the congregation’s outward organization, in order that the sleeping may awake, the wavering be kept from falling, the doubting be established in the truth, and so forth. As a rule, the whole Word of God will find its application within the congregation; at any rate the apostolic Epistles, with their varying content and their many exhortations, were written to Christian congregations.

It almost awakens astonishment, and it ought to awaken horror, that it was so already in the first congregations. Yet God had in a quite particular way governed all things so that the congregation’s outward organization from the beginning should comprise believing people and no others.

What was John the Baptist’s work other than to prepare for the Lord a people made ready, that is, to prepare the congregation by preaching repentance and baptism? Within the people of the old covenant there was by John’s ministry to take place a separation of those who would truly flee from the judgment and receive salvation. And John set as the separating mark the baptism of repentance, with which he baptized those who confessed their sins. And after John had thus worked in Israel in a separating and setting-apart manner, Jesus came, and He did not establish the congregation while the people by the thousands thronged around Him and wanted to make Him king; He waited until persecution and crucifixion and death had frightened away all who had carnal and worldly notions concerning Him and the kingdom. And only when the flock had become so little that it seemed wholly useless as a beginning to the kingdom, only then did Jesus make this little flock into a congregation by sending it His Spirit.

Truly, there is something solemn in this that God has done in order to prepare and bring forth the congregation. And those are not right who teach that this was mere exaggeration and fanaticism, and who think that they know much better what the congregation is and ought to be than John and Jesus and the Spirit of truth Himself.

And yet unworthy, hypocritical people pressed their way into the congregation’s outward organization. And while this fact at the same time speaks loudly of the power of divine truth, in that it shows that man gladly wants to appear to be on the right way, even when he is not, this fact must nevertheless strike us with horror at human falsehood and wickedness, that they will sneak in by dishonest means there where only the highest degree of sincerity and honest acknowledgment of sin gives rightful access.

It is therefore not to be wondered at if in the so-called congregations of our own time also we find many false members. But it is in truth both to be wondered at and to be horrified over, that there are ministers who teach that this is right and proper.

When for centuries one has by the power of the Law, the ban, and the national church forced people into the church (we will not even call such a thing a congregation), then of course it is self-evident that matters must be in a bad state. And matters are in a bad state. Not merely because gross vices are found in the midst of the great majority of congregations, but most of all because one makes no attempt whatever to return to the congregation’s concept and essence.

What then is to be done about it? Fold the hands in the lap and comfort ourselves that we are, after all, not Sodom and Gomorrah?

Is that any comfort? Or is it not written: It shall be more tolerable for Sodom and Gomorrah on the Day of Judgment than for you? Where then is the comfort?

No, what is to be done? First and foremost the same as John the Baptist and Jesus and the apostles did. That is the main thing, and all else is secondary, though it belongs to the work as well.

And we all know what these did; but there is so frightfully little done in following after them.

They preached the Word without favor and without fear; wholly and truly, personally and directly, without respect of persons.

And this is and remains the main thing: light, light, and yet more light in the church. It never becomes too bright, nor bright enough. And upon this the congregation’s true thriving depends. In the light from God, the good thrives, just as surely as the evil fails to thrive. In God’s truth there is nourishment for the souls that seek the truth; in God’s truth there is also cutting judgment over all dishonest and hypocritical hearts. Let the truth sound forth in full, and there shall come both brokenness and resistance, both faith and hardening, both love and hatred.

But the truth is Jesus Christ. Only by a real preaching of Christ does a congregation come into being. Let that sound forth, and the congregation is built and grows and is strengthened; put something else in its place, however popular it may be, and the congregation sinks into a swamp and is corrupted and loses more and more of its true essence and its true strength.

This is the struggle for life in the congregation. It must be kept going all the time, as long as the congregation is here below. It never stops, except where spiritual death takes complete control.

And God, who is rich in mercy, has graciously led and ordered it so that even where everything seems to be death and sleep, He has raised up or sent instruments that have kindled the fire again where it had been extinguished.

But where the proclamation is as it should be, there comes another means in addition, which we also will mention: work.

The congregation lives and exists in order to work. It is a gathering of workers. The congregation is not organized unless there is work for all. It is not a congregation where a gathering of people “hire” a minister to work for them, so that they may be spared it themselves. No, the congregation may in that respect be likened to a choir, which must have a choirmaster to lead and instruct it. Shall the instructor sing alone? Shall not all take part? So it is in the congregation; it is a gathering of God’s servants, men and women, who labor for God’s kingdom, for Christ’s cause. The leader does not become superfluous, but all the more necessary in such a company.

But this work will also help to raise up the congregation and keep it free from worldliness. If all have enough to do with spiritual work and spiritual interests, then worldly elements will surely find the congregation quite a narrow place.

Let the work become so heavy to flesh and blood that it becomes a real cross to bear in Jesus’ footsteps, and it shall help the congregation exceedingly much to be truly a congregation. Then the point will not be to become many in order to make it easy, but the point will be to become living strong, in order to do the Lord’s work with power.

The congregation which will not by these means resist its own becoming worldly will soon lie under, and it will become a disgrace to God’s name instead of unto honor for the Lord and unto light for the world.

When therefore we acknowledge that the congregation must have an outward organization, and at the same time acknowledge that there is occasion for hypocrites to sneak into the organization, then this is the strongest exhortation to be on guard and to use the means the Lord has given us, so that the congregation’s enemies may not become its masters through the congregation’s sloth and unwillingness to take up its cross and bear its burdens. Only the Gospel of the cross and the cross of the Gospel can preserve the congregation, so that it does not become a shameful distortion of Christ’s body and Jesus’ bride.
