STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Indberetning: not merely “report” in a neutral bureaucratic sense; in this context it carries the sense of an official submitted account or report to an annual meeting.
* Aarsmøde: “annual meeting,” but in a church setting it carries ecclesial and deliberative force.
* betydningsfuldt Vendepunkt: more than “important turning point”; it signals a decisive historical and spiritual shift.
* Tilsættelse: here “appointment” or “setting in place,” not casual “adding.”
* Frikirkekomite: must be rendered consistently; “Free Church Committee” is best.
* Begrundelse: not merely “reason,” but “justification” or “grounds.”
* aandelig Brydning: spiritually charged term; “spiritual struggle,” “inner conflict,” or “spiritual contention.” “Brydning” suggests prolonged wrestling, not momentary dispute.
* aandelige Bevægelser: “spiritual movements” or “currents”; should not be flattened into “developments.”
* uforsvarligt: morally and practically indefensible; stronger than “unwise.”
* den gamle Vei: likely “the old way,” with moral-historical force.
* Samfund: dangerous term. In this milieu it can mean church body, organized ecclesial association, denominational society, or religious organization. It should usually be rendered “church body” or “organization” depending on context, but consistently enough to preserve the contrast with “Menighed.”
* Parti / Partiaand / Partitvangen: “party,” “party spirit,” “party compulsion/coercion.” These are polemical ecclesial terms, not modern electoral politics only.
* det uvendelige: “without end,” “endlessly.”
* Omslag: a total reversal, turnabout, or complete change.
* Mangfoldighed: “multiplicity,” not merely “many.”
* Ærgjerrigheder: ambitions, vanities, self-seeking aspirations.
* Menighedernes Frihed: “the freedom of the congregations,” a key theological-ecclesial phrase.
* Tilsidesættelse: “setting aside,” “disregard,” “sidelining.”
* Broderkjærligheden: “brotherly love,” biblically resonant; should not be weakened to “goodwill.”
* Kjød: “the flesh,” not “human nature” if doctrinal edge is to remain.
* tryggere: “safer” or “more secure.”
* Kløgt og Dygtighed: “cleverness and ability/competence”; together they imply confidence in human resourcefulness.
* den gode Del: biblical allusion to Luke 10:42; should be translated with awareness of scriptural source.
* Ufred / Ufrihed: “discord” and “unfreedom/lack of freedom.” “Unfreedom” may sound awkward; “bondage” is too strong in some places. Likely “discord and loss of freedom” or “discord and constraint,” but consistency matters.
* Dannels[e]: formation, founding, constitution of the United Church.
* nytte den samlede Magt til at herske istedetfor til at tjene: “use the united power to rule instead of to serve”; strong ecclesial warning.
* bukkede under: “succumbed,” not merely “failed.”
* paalagde: “charged,” “entrusted,” “laid upon.”
* Menighedsfriheden: central term; “the freedom of the congregation(s).”
* Samarbeidets Kraft: “the strength/power of cooperation.”
* forøges: “be increased.”
* upaatvivleligt: “indubitable,” “beyond doubt,” “unquestionable.”
* politiske Kompromisser: probably “political compromises,” but in an ecclesial context meaning institutional bargaining and expediency.
* Samfundsgjærderne: literally “the fences of the church bodies/organizations”; vivid metaphor that should be preserved.
* lukke inde og lukke ude: “shut in and shut out”; rhythmic parallelism must remain.
* Adskillelsens Mellemvæg: explicit biblical phrase, likely “middle wall of partition” / “dividing wall of separation.” Must preserve scriptural echo, though not in archaic pronouns.
* Lærens Omraade: “the sphere of doctrine.”
* Lærestrid: doctrinal controversy.
* Børnelærdommen: not “childhood learning” in general, but catechetical instruction / the catechism / elementary Christian doctrine taught to children. In this context likely “the Catechism” or “the child’s doctrine,” but since Sverdrup contrasts it with “Præstesatserne,” “the Catechism” is most natural.
* Præstesatserne: “clerical theses/propositions,” a polemical label for theologians’ formulations; should not be softened.
* appellere til det, som er fælles: “appeal to what is common/shared.”
* Forfatningens eller Organisationens Omraade: constitutional or organizational sphere; likely ecclesiastical polity.
* Menighedssagen: “the cause of the congregation” or “the congregational cause”; a programmatic term.
* med fuldt og helt Alvor: “in full and utter earnest.”
* kirkelige Opgaver / Gjøremaal: churchly/ecclesial tasks and undertakings.
* Lægmandsvirksomheden: lay activity / lay work / lay ministry.
* Diakonissesagen: the deaconess cause/work.
* Frikirkens Vei: “the way of the Free Church.”
* maa frem: idiomatic and forceful; “must come forward,” “must be brought to the fore.”
* trives i aandelig Henseende: “thrive spiritually.”
* taale Menighedernes Liv og Frihed: “tolerate/bear the life and freedom of the congregations.”
* Partitvangen og alle deres bitre Følgeevner: “party coercion and all its bitter capacities/consequences.” “Følgeevner” is tricky; sense is its bitter attendant powers/effects.
* fordærves: “is corrupted/ruined.”
* hensynsløs Kamp: “ruthless struggle,” “uncompromising struggle.” In context “uncompromising struggle” may better preserve moral seriousness without suggesting cruelty.
* dyrebarere: “more precious.”
* bare en Vei: “only one way.”
* lægge hele Vægten paa: “lay the whole weight upon,” idiomatically “place the whole emphasis on.”
* af al Magt: “with all one’s might.”
* berettiget, kristeligt og nødvendigt: “justified, Christian, and necessary.” “Christian” here means consonant with Christian duty and character.
* uangribelig: “unassailable.”
* laste os: “blame/reproach us.”
* formaar: “are able.”
* trænges der aandelig Forbindelse: “spiritual connection/fellowship is needed.”
* Fri Samvirken: “free cooperation.”
* Guds Riges Fremme: “the advancement of the Kingdom of God.”
* udvexle Tanker og Aand: literally “exchange thoughts and spirit”; likely “exchange thought and spirit,” preserving elevated diction.
* Kjærlighedens Baand knyttet i Aandens Frihed: “the bonds of love tied in the freedom of the Spirit”; strong biblical-pietistic phrasing.
* frie Møder og Sammenkomster: “free meetings and gatherings.”
* Bestræbelser: efforts/endeavors.
* finde Veien ud: “find the way out.”
* Konstitution: constitution in the formal organizational sense.
* den egentlige Organisation: “the true/proper organization.”
* Samarbeide i den ene Sag: cooperation in the one cause/matter.
* svare sit Kald: “answer its call,” i.e., fulfill its calling.
* repræsentative System: representative system; polemical in context.
* misbrugt: abused/misused.
* interessierer sig: take interest in.
* ofrer noget for den: sacrifice something for it.
* kjærligst pleier den: “care for it most lovingly.”
* ydre Magt: outward/external power.
* aandelig Indflydelse: spiritual influence.
* ydre Baand: outward/external bonds.
* Frivillighedens Vei: “the way of voluntariness” or “the voluntary way.”
* for nødne: “the one thing needful,” another likely biblical echo.
* vække Liv og bevare Liv: “awaken life and preserve life.”
* aandeligt sammenhængende Virke: “spiritually coherent labor/work.”

Syntactic Hazards

* Long cumulative sentences build argument through successive clauses; breaking them into short modern English sentences would flatten the reasoning and weaken the sermonic cadence.
* Repeated infinitive constructions (“at ... at ...”) accumulate pressure and must retain parallelism.
* Semicolon logic is central: the text often pivots from concession to warning, or from diagnosis to principle.
* Parenthetical qualifications (“som vi tør haabe,” “ialfald for vor Del,” “saavidt jeg kan forstaa”) soften tone only slightly and should not be omitted.
* Rhetorical questions carry argument, not merely style; they should remain direct and pointed.
* Triple enumerations (“Det ene ... Det andet ... Det tredie ...”) must stay formally marked.
* Conditional constructions involving “om” and “naar” are morally weighty, often carrying consequence and warning.
* The contrast between “Menighed” and “Samfund” structures the whole passage and must remain grammatically visible.
* Exclamatory force is mostly contained within logic, not punctuation; English should preserve pressure without dramatizing.

Theological Hazards

* Menighed: not merely “community” or “parish,” but congregation as a spiritual, ecclesial reality.
* Menighedens Liv og Frihed: central ecclesiology; must preserve both organic life and spiritual liberty.
* Gud og hans Aand og hans Ord: triadic theological grounding; all three must remain explicit.
* den gode Del: biblical and spiritual evaluation, not casual approval.
* tjene versus herske: service versus domination is both ethical and ecclesial.
* Fred og Frihed: more than peace and civil liberty; these are spiritual-ecclesial goods.
* Adskillelsens Mellemvæg: biblical ecclesial reconciliation image.
* Børnelærdommen: probably the Catechism or foundational Christian teaching; doctrinally central.
* Menighedssagen: not political activism but the theological cause of congregational life.
* Guds Rige: Kingdom of God, not generic religious progress.
* Aandens Frihed: freedom of the Spirit, not individual self-expression.
* Tro paa Guds Aand: faith in God’s Spirit as active agent in church life.
* vække Liv og bevare Liv: regeneration/revival and preservation of spiritual life.
* forene og forbinde de mange smaa Kræfter: image of Spirit-wrought ecclesial unity without coercive institution.

Voice Hazards

* Sverdrup’s tone is solemn, earnest, polemical, and pastoral at once; modern smoothing would erase his authority.
* Irony appears sparingly (“hvad Slags Samfund er det da?”) and must remain sharp, not playful.
* Self-implication (“jeg tilstaar det”) should preserve honesty and moral seriousness.
* Repetition of “Fred og Frihed,” “Menigheden,” and “Samfund” gives the prose its hammering insistence.
* The voice addresses conscience through consequence rather than sentiment; translation must not become inspirational or managerial.
* The strongest lines about conflict (“aldeles hensynsløs Kamp”) must retain severity without melodrama.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “den gode Del” alludes to Luke 10:42, where Mary has chosen “that good part” / “the good portion.” Here it means not merely a prudent choice, but the course truly approved by the Lord. A natural rendering is “the good part from the Lord” or “the good portion from the Lord.” Because the phrase is recognizable and theological, it should retain biblical resonance.
* “Adskillelsens Mellemvæg” echoes Ephesians 2:14. In older English Bibles, especially the KJV, this appears as “the middle wall of partition.” The KJV phrase is strong and apt, but the translation brief instructs against inherited KJV gravity as an overall tone. Still, this specific phrase may naturally be rendered “the middle wall of partition” because it is the established biblical equivalent. “Dividing wall” is more modern but loses some of the theological-literary resonance.
* “Børnelærdommen” likely refers to the Catechism or the elementary Christian doctrine given to children. The contrast with “Præstesatserne” suggests the foundational teaching common to the faithful as against clerically generated theses. Literal “children’s learning” would obscure the ecclesial meaning. “The Catechism” is likely the clearest English rendering.
* “Præstesatserne” appears polemical and probably denotes pastors’ doctrinal theses or formulae. Literal “priest theses” would mislead in a Lutheran context. “Clerical theses” or “pastoral theses” keeps the polemical contrast.
* “Samfundsgjærderne” is metaphorical. Literally “the fences of the church bodies/organizations,” it evokes artificial ecclesiastical boundaries. A literal rendering should be preserved because the metaphor is vivid and governs the following “shut in and shut out.”
* “lukke inde og lukke ude” is a tight rhetorical pair. Literal rendering is best: “shut in and shut out.” It carries institutional exclusion force.
* “maa frem” is idiomatic. Literally “must forward,” meaning “must come to the fore” or “must be brought forward.” “Must come to the fore” preserves the force and public visibility.
* “af al Magt” means “with all one’s might.” This is biblical in ring and should stay vigorous.
* “Kjærlighedens Baand knyttet i Aandens Frihed” is densely pietistic-biblical. It suggests bonds of love formed not by coercion but in the freedom wrought by the Spirit. Literal preservation is preferable.
* “finde Veien ud” means more than solving an organizational puzzle; it implies a providentially right path out of ecclesial confusion. “Find the way out” works well.
* “det ene fornødne” strongly echoes Luke 10:42: “one thing is needful.” This scriptural phrase should be preserved if possible. It adds theological depth to the final appeal to faith.
* “Frivillighedens Vei” could be paraphrased as “voluntary cooperation,” but the image of a “way” matters in this text. “The way of voluntariness” is literal but somewhat stiff; “the voluntary way” is more idiomatic while retaining the principle.
* “svare sit Kald” means to answer or fulfill one’s calling. Literal “answer its call” preserves theological resonance.

Biblical and Pietistic Expressions

* “Gud og hans Aand og hans Ord”: triadic pietistic emphasis on divine agency through Spirit and Word.
* “Fred og Frihed”: recurrent ecclesial watchwords with spiritual and practical dimensions.
* “for kristne Mennesker er Menigheden dyrebarere end Fader og Moder ...” echoes Christ’s demand of supreme loyalty beyond family ties; likely alluding to Matthew 10:37 / Luke 14:26 in softened paraphrase.
* “Guds Riges Fremme hjemme og ude” belongs to missionary and kingdom language common in revivalist contexts.
* “vække Liv og bevare Liv” is classic revival-pietist diction for awakening and sustaining spiritual life.

Where literal translation would obscure force

* “Børnelærdommen” should not be left as “children’s doctrine” unless glossed; “the Catechism” is clearer.
* “Præstesatserne” needs interpretive rendering, likely “clerical theses.”
* “Følgeevner” is obscure if rendered literally. Context suggests something like “its bitter consequences” or “all its bitter attendant powers.”
* “Frivillighedens Vei” may need idiomatic adjustment to “the voluntary way.”

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Opening thesis: The Fargo meeting of June the previous year marked a significant turning point, both for the speaker’s party and, as he hopes, for the church’s development.
* Historical diagnosis: The June 20 decision to appoint a Free Church Committee arose from many years of spiritual struggle and became the starting point for new spiritual movements.
* Negative judgment on the past path: It had become indefensible to continue endlessly forming organization against organization and party against party.
* Warning of consequence: Without a complete reversal, the Lutheran church among their people would be torn into a multiplicity of party organizations driven by petty ambitions and injurious to congregational freedom and brotherly love.
* Temptation acknowledged: Augsburg’s friends could have formed another strong party organization; the speaker confesses that this temptation was real and attractive to the flesh.
* Theological rejection of that temptation: Such a path was not “the good part” from the Lord.
* Retrospective lesson from the United Church: They had sought peace and freedom there, but found instead that centralized organization carries the danger of using united power to rule rather than to serve.
* Practical response: Therefore the Fargo meeting imposed three requirements on the committee: full congregational freedom, strengthened cooperation, and no increase in the number of party organizations.
* Admission of difficulty: The task seemed difficult and nearly impossible.
* Necessity claim: Yet it is unquestionably necessary if there is to be peace and freedom in the church.
* Rejection of false solutions: Neither political compromises nor higher organizational fences can secure peace and freedom.
* Positive program in doctrine: On the doctrinal level, separation has largely been overcome by appealing to what is common, especially as the Catechism has prevailed over clerical theses.
* Transfer of method to polity: What occurred in doctrine must now occur in constitutional/organizational matters.
* Central pivot: Peace and freedom must be sought by taking up the congregational cause in full earnest.
* Empirical support: There are already many churchly tasks that cross organizational fences and proceed without regard to them.
* Interpretive claim: This existing practice shows the true way of the Free Church.
* Core principle: The congregation must come to the fore; work must be done for its life and freedom both within and outside organizations.
* Conditional peace with organizations: There is no war with organizations unless they bring party coercion into the congregations.
* Absolute line: If congregational life is corrupted by organizational politics, there must be uncompromising struggle.
* Ground for severity: For Christians, the congregation is more precious than family; hence there can be no peace with those who harm it.
* Summary principle: A Free Church that is not to become a party has only one path: place the whole weight on the congregation.
* Defense against criticism: Work for the congregation is necessarily justified, Christian, and necessary; criticism can only be that the work is insufficient or poorly done.
* Safeguard for cooperation: To preserve cooperative strength among free congregations, spiritual connection is needed.
* Means: free cooperation, mutual understanding, exchange of thought and spirit, many free meetings and gatherings.
* Application to committee work: These reflections guided the committee in drafting rules.
* Clarification of the proposal: It is not a constitution; the congregation is the true organization.
* Structural principle: The proposal concerns rules for cooperation in the one matter at issue: the congregations’ spiritual life and freedom.
* Further institutional consequence: The representative system is abandoned because in spiritual matters those who care and sacrifice are best suited to understand and tend the cause.
* Resulting polity: External power drops away; the meetings have only spiritual influence and impose no external bond upon congregations.
* Objection anticipated and answered: Can this work? Yes—if the congregations will it; experience already suggests that it can.
* Final theological foundation: One thing is needful—faith in God’s Spirit and power to awaken life, preserve life, create love and freedom, impel work, and unite many small forces into one spiritually coherent labor.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

From the Report to the Annual Meeting of the Lutheran Free Church, 1897.

[Report on the Annual Meeting of the Lutheran Free Church, 1897, p. 12 ff.]

The meeting in Fargo in the month of June last year forms a significant turning point in our development and—as we dare hope—in the development of our Church. The decision of June 20 to appoint a Free Church Committee, and the justification of this decision, was the fruit of a spiritual struggle of many years in our Church, and therefore also the point of departure for new spiritual movements.

Already at that time it had become clear that it was altogether indefensible to continue on the old way, forming church body against church body and party against party without end.

There had to be a change, a complete and entire reversal in the whole tendency of the development, if the Lutheran Church among our people was not to be torn apart into a multiplicity of party organizations, whose number could be reckoned only by the many small ambitions that would try to lift themselves up at the expense of the freedom of the congregations and with disregard for brotherly love.

The friends of Augsburg could have formed a church body in the old style, and with the passionate force of party spirit sought their own cause and strengthened themselves by a truly solid organization according to all the rules of the art. The temptation was great and close at hand; I confess it. It always seems so much safer to our flesh to rely on human cleverness and ability than on God and His Spirit and His Word.

Yet this was not “the good part” from the Lord. Our Church was not served by having the number of party organizations increased and discord and unfreedom grow. For peace and freedom we have labored and suffered through many years; peace and freedom were what we at least, for our part, sought in the formation of The United Church. We did not find them; just when we thought that a good beginning had been made, it became apparent that the very organization of a church body brings with it a danger which at any moment may become a fall, namely, occasion to use the united power to rule instead of to serve. The United Church succumbed to this danger. The point now was to avoid following in the same track, and the meeting in Fargo therefore demanded the following three things of its Free Church Committee when it charged it to draw up rules for a Lutheran Free Church.

The one was that the freedom of the congregation should be fully and entirely acknowledged and preserved without any restriction.

The second was that the strength of cooperation should be strengthened, not slackened.

The third was that the number of party organizations should not be increased.

It seemed a difficult and insurmountable task. And yet it is beyond doubt that this is precisely what is required if there is to be peace and freedom in the Church. We have seen that neither peace nor freedom can be won by political compromises; nor does it help to build the fences of the church bodies higher and higher, and to shut in and shut out according to arbitrary impulses and whims.

What else was there to do than to find a way by which one simply broke down “the middle wall of partition” between the parties, never to raise it again? In the sphere of doctrine, the most essential work in this direction has been done. There can scarcely be said to be any “doctrinal controversy” any more, since the Catechism has in the main prevailed over the clerical theses. By appealing to that which is held in common, the separation in doctrine has been all but overcome. What in this sphere still remains is disagreement in such doctrinal questions as directly concern the congregation and its life and freedom.

What has taken place in the sphere of doctrine must now also take place in the sphere of constitution, or of organization. Peace and freedom must be sought by taking up the cause of the congregation in full and utter earnest.

We already have many ecclesial tasks among us that are not the concern of any one church body, but simply leap over and pass through the fences of the church bodies without even seeing them, or at least without taking account of them. Some of these tasks are carried on by organizations more or less solid, and yet they work. Here one thinks of such ecclesial or Christian undertakings as, for example, the mission to the Jews, the mission to China, lay activity, the Santal mission, the deaconess cause, and in recent years also Augsburg Seminary.

Think for a moment about how this goes on among us, and perhaps it will not be so difficult after all to understand where the way of the Free Church lies.

In reality it is not possible to mistake it. It is the congregation that must come to the fore. For its life and freedom there must be labor in all church bodies and outside all church bodies. If the congregations can thrive spiritually within the church bodies, well and good; if the church bodies can tolerate the life and freedom of the congregations, well and good. We have nothing against that; there is no war between us and the church bodies, unless the church bodies bring party coercion and all its bitter consequences into the congregations. When the congregation and its life are corrupted by church-body politics, then there is struggle, utterly uncompromising struggle. For to Christian people the congregation is more precious than father and mother, than children and kin; and there is no peace between those who love the congregation and those who do it harm, whoever they may be.

If then there is to be a Free Church which is not to become a party, there is only one way: to lay the whole weight upon the congregation. It is precisely as in doctrine, to lay the weight upon the Catechism. It is no party cause. If there is any church body that does not approve of all possible effort being put forth for the congregation and its life, what sort of church body is that? It can hardly be a Christian church body. All church bodies are simply bound to acknowledge that such labor is justified, Christian, and necessary. And when the Free Church has only this one purpose, to work for the life and freedom of the congregation, then to that extent it is unassailable from an ecclesial or Christian side. Then one can—rightly—reproach us only that we do not labor enough, or do not labor in the best way. And we must indeed rejoice if anyone will help us to do it better, and most of all if those who fault us will themselves do the necessary work so much better than we are able.

But if free, independent congregations are not to lose anything of the strength of cooperation, then spiritual fellowship is needed. Free cooperation in everything that may serve to advance the Kingdom of God at home and abroad is necessary and beneficial; if this is to take place, then we must understand one another and know one another. We must exchange thought and spirit and thus have the bonds of love tied in the freedom of the Spirit. So far as I can understand, a truly free-church work will be almost impossible under our circumstances without very many free meetings and gatherings.

Such and the like have been the considerations that have also guided your Free Church Committee in its work with the proposed rules. These are now laid before you with the hope that they may be of some use in our efforts to find the way out.

It is not a proposal for a constitution. We proceed from the fact that the congregation is the true organization. These are only rules for cooperation in the one matter with which we are concerned, namely, the spiritual life and freedom of the congregations, so that they may better answer their calling and carry out their tasks.

We have therefore also given up the representative system, which among the so-called church bodies among us has been so abused. We have proceeded from the fact that in spiritual matters, those who take an interest in a cause and sacrifice something for it are also those who best understand it and most lovingly care for it.

With that, outward power also falls away altogether. Our free-church meetings have only spiritual influence, without any kind of outward bond upon the individual congregations.

Will such a thing then be able to go forward? Yes, why not, if only the congregations will it? We have labored on this voluntary way now for some years, and we cannot complain that it does not go. The rules now proposed aim at letting voluntariness continue to govern.

But in this connection one thing is the one thing needful: faith, faith in God’s Spirit, faith in His power to awaken life and preserve life, give love, create freedom, drive to labor, unite and bind the many small forces into one spiritually coherent work.

(Georg Sverdrup.)
