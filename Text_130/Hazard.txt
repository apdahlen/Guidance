STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Menighedernes: not merely “churches” in an institutional or building sense, but “the congregations’,” referring to gathered ecclesial bodies.
* Ansvar: not just generic “responsibility,” but moral accountability with spiritual and ecclesial implications.
* Samfundshandlinger: literally “society-actions” or “synod-actions”; in context, corporate acts/decisions of the church body. A loose rendering such as “community work” would distort the polemical and constitutional sense.
* Indremission: a pietistic and ecclesial term meaning “home mission” or “inner mission,” not “internal mission” in a merely administrative sense.
* paavist: “shown,” “demonstrated,” “pointed out”; it carries argumentative force.
* Partisag: “party matter,” “factional cause,” “partisan matter”; the political/factional nuance must remain.
* fradømme: not merely “deny,” but “pronounce away from,” “deprive of by judgment,” suggesting a condemnatory verdict.
* Kristenliv: not abstract “Christianity,” but lived Christian life, spiritual vitality, actual Christian life.
* Budskabet om Frelsen i Kristus: “the message of salvation in Christ”; doctrinally weighty and evangelical.
* aandelig døde: “spiritually dead,” a strongly theological phrase, not merely inactive or indifferent.
* forvendte: perverted, warped, turned awry; morally and spiritually disordered.
* bryder sig om: “care about,” but in context with moral seriousness.
* laster: “blames,” “censures,” “finds fault with”; stronger than casual criticism.
* ukristelig: “unchristian,” not simply unpleasant or unhelpful.
* billigt: here “fair,” “reasonable,” “proper,” not “cheap.”
* bryr sig om, hvad man tager paa sig: concern for what obligations one assumes; has moral seriousness, not casual preference.
* retskaffel: likely older/variant form for “retskaffen,” meaning upright, honest, just.
* en for alle og alle for en ansvarlige: evokes complete mutual liability, both rhetorical and practical.
* tilintetgjør: “annihilates,” “destroys utterly”; must retain force.
* Udvei: “way out,” “escape,” “recourse.”
* Vidnesbyrd: “testimony,” “witness”; often with Christian and moral force.
* Feiltrin: “misstep,” “false step,” with moral and ecclesial implications.
* Fordømmelsesdom: “sentence of condemnation,” a juridical-theological term.
* taales: “be tolerated,” “be borne.”
* Solidariteten: solidarity, corporate mutual implication; in this polemical context it is nearly covenantal or constitutional in force.
* sterkt Vidnesbyrd: “strong testimony” or “powerful evidence.”
* Skyld: guilt, culpability, blame.
* syndige Strid: “sinful strife,” not merely unfortunate conflict.
* ufeilbar: “infallible,” crucial irony.
* oprigtigt: sincerely, genuinely.
* yderlig fornærmet: deeply/extremely offended.
* ridsk Kritik: likely “harsh,” “sharp,” “severe” criticism; must preserve severity without overmodernizing.
* Irettesættelse: reproof, correction, rebuke.
* formaasteligt: presumptuous, overbold in a morally culpable sense.
* vil gjerne have det saaledes: “would gladly have it so”; suggests willing preference for the arrangement.
* slaa sig til Ro: settle down complacently, rest content.
* Forvisningen: assurance, conviction.
* frisindede: broad-minded, liberal-minded, independent-minded; contextually closer to free-minded or independent in judgment, not necessarily doctrinally liberal in a modern sense.
* tilovers endda: still left over, remaining nonetheless.
* forbeholde sig: reserve to oneself, insist on retaining.
* Overbevisning: conviction.

Syntactic Hazards

* Long accumulative sentences build argument by successive clauses; shortening them too much would flatten the polemical logic.
* Repeated rhetorical questions create pressure and expose the opposing view’s implications; they should remain as questions.
* Semicolon logic and colon introductions are structurally important and should be preserved where possible.
* Quoted material from “Lutheraneren” must remain distinct in voice while fitting the flow of the editorial response.
* Parallel formulations such as “med sin Person, sin Sjæl, sit Legeme og sit Gods” must remain cumulative and formal.
* Conditional constructions (“skal det gjælde…”, “om Samfundet…”) drive reductio ad absurdum reasoning and should not be weakened.
* The argument pivots from citation, to inference, to testing implications, to ironic conclusion; this must remain visible.

Theological Hazards

* Kristenliv must retain its sense of real spiritual life before God, not nominal religion.
* Budskabet om Frelsen i Kristus must remain explicitly Christological and soteriological.
* aandelig døde is doctrinally charged language echoing biblical categories of spiritual death.
* syndige Strid must remain morally and spiritually condemning.
* Vidnesbyrd, Irettesættelse, Fordømmelsesdom all carry ecclesial and moral weight.
* The issue of collective responsibility touches conscience, guilt, and the relation between individual believers, congregations, and church bodies.
* The contrast between external corporate decisions and the inward reality of Christian life is the doctrinal center of the piece.

Voice Hazards

* Sverdrup’s tone is sharp, controlled, ironic, and morally serious; smoothing it into neutral academic prose would erase the voice.
* His irony depends on apparently granting an opponent’s premise and then exposing its consequences; this must remain intact.
* He speaks not as a detached analyst but as one pressing conscience and public accountability.
* The closing contrast between complacent majoritarian assurance and retained personal conviction must preserve its sting.
* The prose should remain earnest and formal, not conversational or journalistic in a modern sense.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “bringe Budskabet om Frelsen i Kristus videre” means to carry the Gospel onward to others. It implies evangelical transmission, not merely passing along information.
* “bære det kristne Navn” means to bear the Christian name, echoing biblical and ecclesial language about confession and identity. “Call themselves Christian” is possible, but “bear the Christian name” better preserves the solemn register.
* “en for alle og alle for en ansvarlige” adapts a familiar solidarity formula into the register of liability and accountability. The rhetorical force lies in applying total mutual obligation to synodical decisions.
* “Staar og falder … med” means “stands or falls with”; this is a fixed expression indicating total dependence.
* “tage denne Sag alvorligt” means to take the matter seriously, with moral and practical consequence.
* “slaa sig til Ro med Forvisningen om” means to rest content in the assurance that; it implies complacency.
* “vil gjerne have det saaledes” means more than preference; it suggests being quite willing to accept the arrangement because it is convenient.
* “have Ret og ikke kan tage Feil” preserves a near-absolute confidence: to be right and unable to be mistaken.
* “forbeholde sig at have sin egen Overbevisning og sit eget Ansvar” is not individualism in a modern expressive sense; it means retaining before God and conscience one’s own conviction and responsibility.

Biblical allusions and pietistic expressions

* “Kristenliv” reflects pietistic emphasis on living faith and the actual life of the Christian, not mere formal membership.
* “Budskabet om Frelsen i Kristus” echoes New Testament proclamation language regarding salvation in Christ.
* “aandelig døde” strongly recalls biblical categories such as being dead in sins.
* “Vidnesbyrd” and “Irettesættelse” fit a scriptural and devotional culture where testimony and reproof are moral duties.
* “bære det kristne Navn” resonates with biblical naming and confession.

King James-style natural equivalents to note, without adopting archaic pronouns

* “bear the Christian name” is a natural elevated equivalent.
* “spiritually dead” is the most direct doctrinal equivalent.
* “stands and falls with” preserves biblical-rhetorical cadence.
* “message of salvation in Christ” is preferable to any flattened paraphrase.

Expressions where literal translation would obscure force

* Samfundshandlinger should not be translated mechanically as “social actions.” In context it refers to acts or decisions of the church body.
* frisindede should not simply become “liberal” without qualification; that would import later political or theological meanings. “Independent-minded” or “free-minded” fits better in context.
* billigt must not become “cheaply.”
* tilovers endda should not be rendered literally in a clumsy way; “still remain” or “are still left” preserves sense.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis introduction: The editorial recalls an earlier piece arguing that the Ole Nilsen decision made church mission into a partisan matter.
* Opposing claim introduced: “Lutheraneren” replies that to accuse the synod in this way is effectively to deny Christian life to all its congregations.
* Immediate inference: The editorial draws out the implication that “Lutheraneren” makes every congregation and member strongly responsible for the decision.
* First reductio: If criticizing the decision equals denying Christian life to 700 congregations, then solidarity has been exaggerated beyond what is proper.
* Testing implications: The author asks whether every congregation and member is personally liable for all annual meeting decisions, even in financial matters or debt.
* Spiritual implication tested: If the synod makes an unchristian decision, does that really annihilate the Christian life of all members, including those who opposed it?
* Further reductio: If so, then any criticism of a synodical decision is necessarily a condemnation of all congregations.
* Explanatory turn: On this logic, suppression of delegates and expulsion of congregations becomes understandable, because criticism would be treated as a total condemnation.
* Deeper diagnosis: This exaggerated solidarity is presented as evidence that the majority sincerely regards itself as without guilt in the sinful conflict, effectively as infallible in its own eyes.
* Final appeal to consequence: If congregations truly hold both that they are morally responsible for everything the annual meeting decides and that criticism is sinful presumption, then some others will still reserve their own conviction and responsibility, even at the cost of expulsion.
* Concluding force: The piece ends by contrasting complacent majoritarian comfort with conscientious dissent.

STEP 4: BIBLICAL ECHO IDENTIFICATION

* “Kristenliv” echoes pietistic and New Testament distinctions between living faith and dead formalism.
* “Budskabet om Frelsen i Kristus” likely echoes apostolic Gospel proclamation, especially Pauline language of salvation in Christ.
* “aandelig døde” strongly echoes Ephesians 2:1 and related passages about being dead in sins.
* “bære det kristne Navn” may echo the scriptural motif of bearing the Lord’s name or being called by His name; compare Acts 11:26 and broader biblical naming theology.
* “Vidnesbyrd” and “Irettesættelse” echo biblical duties of testimony, reproof, and correction; compare prophetic and apostolic modes of admonition.
* “Staar og falder” has biblical-rhetorical resonance, though it may also function idiomatically here.
* “sin Person, sin Sjæl, sit Legeme og sit Gods” resembles older totalizing formulas of consecration or liability, though not a direct quotation.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The warnings and rhetorical questions must remain pointed and uncompromising.
* Irony should be preserved through controlled phrasing, not exaggerated sarcasm.
* Terms of moral judgment such as “unchristian,” “sinful,” “presumptuous,” “condemnation,” and “spiritually dead” must remain strong.
* The prose should implicate the reader in questions of conscience and responsibility.
* The closing sentence must retain quiet defiance rather than emotional protest.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “maa” should usually remain “must,” not “may have to” or “would have to,” unless English idiom requires adjustment.
* “skal det gjælde” should retain normative force: “if it is to hold” or “if it is to apply.”
* “vil da” in rhetorical questions should preserve practical consequence: “will then.”
* “kan ingen” should remain categorical: “no one can.”
* “kan tage Feil” should become “can be mistaken” or “can err,” preserving impossibility.
* “ikke taales” must remain strong: “cannot be tolerated” or “is not tolerated.”

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Menighed / Menigheder = congregation / congregations
* Samfund = synod or church body; in this piece “synod” best conveys a federated church body making annual decisions
* Aarsmødet = annual meeting
* Kristenliv = Christian life
* Frelsen i Kristus = salvation in Christ
* aandelig døde = spiritually dead
* ukristelig = unchristian
* syndig / syndige = sinful
* Vidnesbyrd = testimony or witness; where moral-public force is central, “testimony” is preferable
* Irettesættelse = reproof
* Fordømmelsesdom = sentence of condemnation / judgment of condemnation
* Ansvar = responsibility, accountability depending on context; “responsibility” can remain dominant, with “liable” used in testing questions
* Overbevisning = conviction

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* “bære det kristne Navn” must remain metaphorical, not flattened into mere self-identification.
* “aandelig døde” must remain vivid and theological, not reduced to “spiritually inactive.”
* “Staar og falder” must remain an image of total dependence.
* “slaa sig til Ro” should preserve the image of settling into complacent rest.
* “udstødt” should remain “cast out” or “expelled,” preserving ecclesial exclusion.

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* The piece moves from quotation, to sober inference, to relentless questioning, to irony, to grave warning, to a final note of conscientious resistance.
* Severity dominates, but there is also restrained moral clarity rather than rage.
* The final sentence introduces firmness and resolve against institutional pressure; this contrast must remain visible.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* The translation should allow long sentences to unfold in measured stages.
* Repeated rhetorical questions should sound fit for public reading.
* Clause chains should preserve breath and pressure, especially in the central paragraphs.
* Semicolons and colons should be retained where they carry argumentative cadence.
* The final cadence should close with moral steadiness rather than flourish.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

On the Congregations’ Responsibility for Synodical Acts.

[Editorial article in “Folkebladet” for February 24, 1897.]

Some time ago there was printed in “Folkebladet” a piece on “The Free-Church Inner Mission.” It pointed out what effect the Ole Nilsen Decision had upon the synod’s mission work, namely, to make it a partisan matter.

On that occasion “Lutheraneren,” in its issue of February 4 of this year, writes:

“No darker accusation can be brought against a synod of congregations. It amounts to nothing less than to pronounce all these congregations devoid of all Christian life. For where there is Christian life, there it always stands also as a principal task to carry the message of salvation in Christ onward to those who do not know it.

If, then, The United Church, which consists of more than 700 congregations, has something else as its principal task, then at any rate the greater part of these 700 congregations must not only be spiritually dead, but so perverted that they do not even care to bear the Christian name.”

From this one may thus see that “Lutheraneren” straightforwardly makes all the congregations of the synod, and all the individual members of them, responsible for the Ole Nilsen Decision to so great a degree that the relation becomes this: if anyone censures this decision and its effect upon The United Church’s missionary work, then that is the same as “pronouncing all the 700 congregations devoid of all Christian life.”

So strong, in the opinion of that organ, is the solidarity in The United Church: if the Ole Nilsen Decision is unchristian, then the synod is unchristian, and then there is no Christian life in 700 congregations?

This claim concerning responsibility goes farther, it seems to us, than is fair. But if within The United Church it is to hold that each congregation and each congregation member is responsible, with person, soul, body, and goods, for all that the annual meeting resolves, then it is a question whether it will be possible for any congregation or person to remain with it, if one otherwise cares what one takes upon oneself.

If the synod adopts a very upright decision in money matters, incurs a vast debt, or the like, will then each congregation and each congregation member be liable, one for all and all for one?

And if the synod adopts an unchristian decision, is that then something which really annihilates the Christian life of all the individual congregation members?

Is there no way out for those who vote against it, testify against it, and work against it either?
Does the Christian life of all the members stand and fall with a single synodical decision?

Yes, so it appears, if the claim of “Lutheraneren” is correct, that he who censures a synodical decision concerning inner mission thereby pronounces 700 congregations devoid of all Christian life.

If the matter of the congregations’ responsibility for the annual meeting’s decisions hangs together in this way, then it is altogether understandable why The United Church excludes delegates and casts out congregations which oppose a proposal or a decision. For if every testimony concerning the annual meeting’s misstep is a complete sentence of condemnation over all congregations and congregation members, then it is altogether natural that such a testimony is not tolerated.

For us this understanding of solidarity is a new and strong testimony that the majority means it sincerely in its declaration that its conduct up to 1895 has been without guilt in the sinful strife in the synod. For only a majority infallible in its own eyes can feel itself so deeply offended by harsh criticism or reproof.

Will the congregations now take this matter seriously and really hold fast to these two things: that, first, they are responsible for all that the annual meeting determines, so that the annual meeting’s decision carries the same moral responsibility for them as if they themselves had determined it;

and that, second, it is sinful and presumptuous for any congregation or any congregation member to oppose or criticize any synodical decision, because by doing so they pronounce 700 congregations devoid of all Christian life?

It is very possible that some congregations would gladly have it so. It is quite convenient to settle down in the assurance that, being so many, one must be right and cannot be mistaken.

But then one must not be surprised either that there still remain some independent-minded congregations and congregation members who will reserve to themselves their own conviction and their own responsibility, and be cast out, if that is not tolerated.

[Georg Sverdrup.]
