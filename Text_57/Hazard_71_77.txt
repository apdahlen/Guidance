STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* Menighedernes Sammensetning: not merely “composition” in a statistical sense, but the make-up, constitution, and actual human/spiritual constituency of the congregations.
* Menighed: in this context best rendered consistently as “congregation” rather than “church” where the visible local body is in view; but the term also carries the broader theological sense of the gathered Christian community.
* Hedninger: “Gentiles,” not merely “pagans,” because of the New Testament Jew/Gentile frame.
* Syndebyrden: “the burden of sin,” not generic guilt alone; it suggests an oppressive spiritual load.
* Helvedsfrugten: literally the fruit or consequence of hell; idiomatically “hell’s fruit” is awkward, so the sense is the damnable consequence or issue of sin. It must retain eschatological severity.
* bære hans Kors: “bear His cross,” with explicit discipleship resonance; not to be weakened into “accept hardship.”
* Brødre, Disciple, Hellige: New Testament designations, doctrinally charged; “brothers,” “disciples,” “saints.”
* Klengenavn: “nickname” is too weak; “term of reproach” or “derisive name” better preserves hostility.
* kristelige Erfaring: not merely “Christian experience” in a private devotional sense, but lived ecclesial and spiritual knowledge.
* bærer Frugt i Taalmodighed: Scriptural phrase; “bears fruit with patience” or “in perseverance.” The biblical echo must remain audible.
* Kristi Liv arter sig kraftigt: “the life of Christ manifests itself powerfully” rather than merely “appears strongly.”
* Surdeig: “leaven,” with biblical and doctrinal resonance.
* Kjødelighedens Aand: “the spirit of carnality” or “fleshliness,” not merely “worldliness.”
* enfoldige: in older devotional prose often “simple” in the sense of sincere, single-hearted, guileless, not unintelligent.
* Hævdsret: an established customary right; here metaphorically, an error repeated so long that it has nearly acquired legitimacy.
* Aandsfylde: “fullness of the Spirit.”
* Troesfreidighed: difficult; suggests boldness/confidence of faith, not mere cheerfulness.
* slaat sig tiltaals: “comforted themselves” or “quieted themselves”; implies self-soothing complacency.
* opofrende og missionerende: “self-sacrificing and missionary” or “given to mission.”
* Kjævlerier: quarrels, wranglings; colloquial edge should be preserved.
* gjængse Farisæisme: prevalent/current Phariseeism, not simply “Pharisaism” in abstract.
* retfærdiggjort af Troen uden Lovens Gjerninger: Pauline doctrinal formula; must remain precise.
* Gudsdyrkelse: “worship,” here specifically a false religious devotion.
* Askeſe / Selvforsagelse: “asceticism / self-denial”; the former is technical, the latter more devotional.
* verdslig Visdom: “worldly wisdom,” Pauline contrast to the Gospel.
* vanhellige Fabler: “profane fables” or “godless myths.”
* kjødelig Fordel: “carnal advantage,” not merely material benefit.
* Anledning for Kjødet: Pauline phrase, “occasion for the flesh.”
* Herskesygens Aand: “the spirit of domination” or “lust to rule”; must preserve moral judgment.
* Vindings Skyld: “for filthy gain” would sound too KJV-marked; “for the sake of gain” preserves force without archaism.
* alslags grove Laster: “all manner of gross vices.”
* Lemmer: “members,” with bodily/ecclesial undertone.
* udvortes Ordning: “outward ordering” or “external order”; not merely organization.
* Afvigelser og Frafald: “deviations and defections/apostasies.”
* Evangeliearbeide: “Gospel work.”
* dvæle: “dwell” or “linger” briefly; rhetorical pacing matters.
* Tilstanden: “condition” or “state.”
* Partisjngen: evidently partisplittelsen / party-spirit; should be rendered “party divisions” or “factionalism.”
* Uterlighedssynder: sexual immorality / licentious sins.
* Tugt: church discipline, not generic upbringing.
* Havesyge: greed or covetousness.
* Proceslyst: litigiousness.
* Nadverd: Lord’s Supper.
* Klassehøi: class pride / social hauteur.
* Tungemaalen: tongues.
* Profetien: prophecy.
* indtrængte Brødre: intrusive / false brethren who have pushed their way in.
* Øretuden: whisperings / tale-bearing; a vivid vice term.
* Opblæsthed: puffed-up pride.
* Hovori: likely “hordom” / fornication in older spelling context; the vice-catalogue force must be preserved.
* Lunkenheds i det kristelige Barmhjertighedsarbeide: lukewarmness in Christian works of mercy.
* annammede: received.
* Kar til Ære og Kar til Vanære: vessels unto honor and unto dishonor; strong biblical echo.
* Besnærelse: entanglement, ensnarement, seductive power of the world.
* grebet det: likely echo of Phil. 3, “attained” / “laid hold of it.”
* Bedrøvelse: sorrow, specifically godly sorrow where context suggests repentance.
* blive istand: be enabled / be made able.
* Bispekirke / Præstesamfund: episcopal church / priestly clerical order; institutional contrast must remain sharp.
* Fremstilling eller Realisation af Kirkens Enhed: manifestation or realization of the Church’s unity.

Syntactic Hazards

* The prose proceeds by long accumulative sentences with semicolon pivots and doctrinal build-up. Breaking these into short modern sentences would flatten the argument and weaken the sermonic force.
* Repeated adversative patterns: “Men...,” “Og...,” “Thi...,” “Derfor...” guide logic and exhortation; they must remain visible.
* Lists of corruptions, warnings, and examples are cumulative and rhetorical, not merely informational.
* Parenthetical biblical references are woven into the argument and should not be detached from it.
* The author often uses contrastive structures: first ideal appearance, then actual corruption, then doctrinal correction.
* Rhetorical questions (“Thi hvad finder vi...?” “Hvorledes var nu derefter...?”) advance the sermon-like movement and should be preserved.
* Appositional expansions after a noun or clause create interpretive force; they should not be prematurely simplified.

Theological Hazards

* The opening description of entry into the congregation presumes conversion: liberation from sin’s burden and hell’s consequence through faith in Jesus’ blood, followed by readiness to bear His cross.
* The discussion of justification by faith without works of the Law must remain precisely Pauline and not be blurred into general Protestant sentiment.
* The contrast between grace alone and additions such as angel worship, asceticism, worldly wisdom, myths, and speculative teaching is doctrinally central.
* The treatment of congregation includes both visible order and spiritual life; reducing it either to invisible spirituality or to external institution would distort the argument.
* The final paragraphs hold together imperfection and real spiritual life: the congregation is not perfect, yet it has the Spirit’s witness of sin, righteousness, and judgment. This triad is Johannine and doctrinally weighty.
* Daily sorrow and daily conversion/repentance to God must remain active, ongoing, and ecclesial.
* The Church’s unity is presented as concretely realized among congregations without episcopal hierarchy or clerical caste; this ecclesiology must remain clear.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is polemical, corrective, and earnest, yet not shrill. Excessive smoothing would erase the Lutheran-sermonic gravity.
* The author rebukes hypocrisy, complacency, romanticizing of the apostolic age, and excuses for dead mass congregations. These rebukes must remain sharp.
* The diction often moves from descriptive to accusatory to exhortational in one flow; this pressure on the reader must be preserved.
* Irony is present in phrases such as “med tilsyneladende Ret” and in the exposure of false consolation; it should not be flattened.
* The author’s praise of the Corinthians is intentionally paradoxical after severe criticism; that tension must remain intact.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* “bærer Frugt i Taalmodighed” echoes Luke 8:15. The sense is not mere patience in a general moral sense, but persevering endurance under which the implanted word bears fruit. “Bear fruit with perseverance” gives the clearest biblical sense.
* “Begyndt er ikke endt” is proverbial and admonitory: what has begun is not therefore completed. It warns against premature confidence in spiritual beginnings.
* “Farisæernes Surdeig og Saducæernes Surdeig” is a Gospel allusion to corrupting influence. “Leaven” is the natural biblical equivalent in English.
* “blive ‘retfærdiggjort af Troen uden Lovens Gjerninger’” alludes to Romans 3:28. The formula should remain close to standard Pauline English: “justified by faith without the works of the Law.”
* “Anledning for Kjødet” is a Pauline expression from Galatians 5:13. A natural equivalent is “an occasion for the flesh.”
* “herske over Hjorden” echoes 1 Peter 5:3. “Lord it over the flock” would be a strong biblical equivalent, but it risks KJV coloration. “Rule over the flock” is plainer; “domineer over the flock” is interpretive. “Lord it over the flock” may still be best if used sparingly because it most exactly captures the biblical echo.
* “Kar til Ære og Kar til Vanære” echoes Romans 9 and 2 Timothy 2. “Vessels unto honor and vessels unto dishonor” preserves resonance, though “unto” slightly elevates diction. “For honor / for dishonor” is more neutral. Because the passage is overtly biblical, “unto” is justified without using archaic pronouns.
* “hverken var den fuldkommen ei heller havde den grebet det. Men den jagede derefter” echoes Philippians 3:12. The sense is not merely moral striving but eschatological pressing on toward what is not yet attained.
* “Vidnesbyrd om Synd og Retfærdighed og Dom” echoes John 16:8. This should be translated transparently as “witness/testimony concerning sin and righteousness and judgment.” “Conviction” is interpretive; “testimony” better matches the wording and theological texture.
* “slumre ind enten i Syndesikkerhed” expresses complacent security in sin. Literal “fall asleep in security of sin” is awkward. Better: “sink into security in sin” or “slumber into security in sin.” The latter preserves imagery but may sound odd; “sink into sinful security” preserves sense and force.
* “slaa sig tiltaals” means to pacify oneself inwardly. Literal rendering would obscure the self-excusing spiritual complacency.
* “det var en anden Sag i gamle Dage” is an excuse-making idiom: things were different then, so present deficiency is excusable.
* “en hel Hob sove” uses “sleep” for death, echoing Pauline language; this should be preserved.
* “Giver os Indgang!” idiomatically means “Make room for us in your hearts” in 2 Corinthians 7:2. Because the author is quoting Scripture, the best English should reflect that accepted biblical sense.
* “med Frygt og Bæven” is a scriptural phrase, naturally “with fear and trembling.”
* King James-style equivalents that are natural without imposing full KJV tone include: “leaven,” “bear fruit,” “justified by faith,” “works of the Law,” “occasion for the flesh,” “fear and trembling,” “vessels unto honor.”

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis: The New Testament writings clearly show both the composition and the further inner development of the apostolic congregations.
* Initial claim: The congregations were founded first among Jews, then among Gentiles, and in either case consisted of believing men and women only.
* Ground for that claim: there was originally no reason for anyone to join except genuine deliverance from sin through faith in Jesus’ blood and willingness to bear His cross.
* Naming claim: accordingly they were called brothers, disciples, and saints; “Christians” was at first only a nickname/term of reproach; yet from the first they were collectively called the congregation.
* Major corrective pivot: however, Christian experience and the New Testament show that not all that is sown bears fruit unto perseverance.
* Development of corruption: Satan works most vigorously where Christ’s life appears strongly; Pharisaic and Sadducean leaven soon began its work.
* Main corrective assertion: even within the New Testament period, all congregations without exception were infected by carnality and far from consisting exclusively of simple and living Christians.
* Polemical application: to hide this fact is either ignorance of Scripture or hypocrisy hardened by repetition into accepted doctrine.
* Exposure of false idealization: people romanticize the first congregations as perfect and thereby excuse the dead mass-congregations of the present.
* Counter-assertion: if the congregation is the work of God’s Spirit, present congregations ought at least to become as living, bold in faith, patient, self-sacrificing, and missionary as those of the apostolic age.
* Evidence section: examples from Acts and the epistles—Ananias and Sapphira, quarrels, false teachings, legalism, angel worship, asceticism, worldly wisdom, myths, denial of resurrection, misuse of Christ’s return, greed, fleshly abuse of freedom, falsehood, domination, vice lists, and ecclesial disorders.
* Ecclesiological clarification: for Paul and the other New Testament writers, the congregation is not an invisible, indeterminate aggregate of believers wherever found, but a definite visible circle with outward order.
* Supporting evidence for visibility: Paul greets named persons, families, and whole congregations; he knows them concretely.
* Case study transition: therefore the Corinthian congregation is examined more closely.
* Corinth evidence: numerous grave faults—factionalism, immorality, failure of discipline, greed, lawsuits, abuse of the Supper, class pride, pride in gifts, false teaching, lukewarm mercy, false brothers, intrigue against Paul.
* Broadening claim: the other congregations also seem to have had serious defects.
* Counterbalancing evidence: yet Paul still thanks God for the Corinthians and expresses deep confidence, love, comfort, and joy in them because their sorrow led to repentance.
* Concluding synthesis: the first apostolic congregations were mixed; there were vessels unto honor and unto dishonor. They were not perfect, but they had the life of God’s Spirit and an unceasing witness concerning sin, righteousness, and judgment.
* Final consequence: precisely thus they were able to carry on the work of the Gospel in the world, in weakness yet sincerity, and to realize the unity of the Church without forming either an episcopal church or a clerical order.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The translation must retain the author’s rebuke of sentimental idealization of the apostolic church.
* The phrases exposing hypocrisy, falsehood, self-excusing complacency, and dead mass-congregations must remain severe.
* The cumulative vice-catalogues and ecclesial failures should not be softened into abstract shortcomings.
* The final commendation of the apostolic congregations must not erase their defects; rather, the paradox is the point: true congregational life exists amid conflict, discipline, sorrow, and repentance.
* The pastoral force lies in consequence, not emotional embellishment. The translation should sound morally inescapable, not dramatic.

STEP 6: MODAL FORCE PRESERVATION

* “skulde” in contexts of reason, expectation, or obligation should often remain “should,” not weakened to “might.”
* “bør” in “vore Menigheder idetmindste blive...” must remain strong: “ought at least to become...” not merely “could.”
* “maa” in excuse-making phrases should retain necessity where present.
* Negative modal force such as “ei heller” should remain firm and explicit.
* The practical consequence sections should retain the force of necessity and theological obligation.

STEP 7: CONSISTENT THEOLOGICAL TERM RENDERING

* Menighed / Menigheder: “congregation / congregations” throughout when referring to local visible bodies.
* kristelig / kristne: “Christian.”
* Tro / Troen: “faith.”
* Naade / Naaden: “grace.”
* retfærdiggjort: “justified.”
* Kjødet / kjødelig / Kjødelighed: “the flesh / fleshly / carnality.”
* Aand / Guds Aand / Kristi Aand: “Spirit / God’s Spirit / Christ’s Spirit.”
* Omvendelse: “repentance” where the result is emphasized, “turning to God” where the motion is emphasized; here mostly “repentance” except in the final sentence where “daily turning to God” may preserve movement.
* Hellighed: “holiness.”
* Vidnesbyrd: “witness” or “testimony”; use “witness” consistently in the final synthesis.
* Menighedens Lemmer: “members of the congregation.”
* Nadverd: “the Lord’s Supper.”
* Evangeliets Gjerning / Evangeliearbeide: “the work of the Gospel / Gospel work.”
* Kirke / Kirkens Enhed: “Church / the unity of the Church.”

STEP 8: METAPHOR INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

* Leaven imagery must remain “leaven,” not be paraphrased as “influence.”
* “Burden of sin” must remain metaphorical and concrete.
* “Bear His cross” must remain intact.
* “Vessels unto honor and unto dishonor” must remain intact.
* “Sleep” for death should remain intact.
* “The congregation ... surrounded and permeated by the world’s sin and entanglement” preserves the original image of immersion and penetration.
* “slumre ind” should preserve sleep imagery where possible.
* “jagede derefter” should preserve pursuit imagery: “pressed on after it.”

STEP 9: TONE CONTRAST PRESERVATION

* Preserve the movement from ideal founding purity to subsequent corruption.
* Preserve the shift from critique of present excuses to constructive exhortation.
* Preserve the severe catalogue of Corinthian faults alongside Paul’s heartfelt thanksgiving and affection.
* Preserve the final movement from imperfection to real spiritual vitality and ecclesial unity.

STEP 10: ORAL CADENCE AWARENESS

* Keep major paragraphs in long periodic sentences where the original reasons by accumulation.
* Preserve repeated connective openings such as “But,” “And,” “For,” “Therefore.”
* Retain rhetorical questions as turning-points.
* Allow lists to build weight through coordination rather than reducing them to clipped prose.
* Keep the ending spacious and cumulative, as it functions like a sermonic conclusion.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

The Composition of the Congregations

and their further inner development in the apostolic age also emerges clearly enough from the New Testament writings. At first they consisted of Jews alone, afterward also, and in many places exclusively, of Gentiles from every nation. But whether the congregations were founded among Jews or Gentiles, they consisted of believing men and women and nothing else. Indeed, from the beginning there was no reason whatever why anyone should join the congregation unless they had been delivered from the burden of sin and the fruit of hell through faith in Jesus’ blood and therefore were ready to bear His cross. Therefore, too, in the beginning they were called “brothers,” “disciples,” and “saints,” and only received the name Christians as a term of reproach. But all together they were called, from the first moment until now, the congregation.

But as Christian experience teaches us to this very day, both with respect to individuals and to congregations, not all that is sown bears fruit with perseverance. What is begun is not ended, and Satan is never more active than where the life of Christ manifests itself powerfully. The leaven of the Pharisees and the leaven of the Sadducees soon began their baleful work in the first congregations. And thus, as we actually find the congregations, even within the period of the New Testament itself, they were without exception all infected by the spirit of carnality and very far from consisting exclusively of simple and living Christians.

To hide this fact from oneself proceeds either from complete ignorance of God’s Word in this matter, or from hypocrisy and falsehood, which through centuries of repetition have almost attained prescriptive right as a legitimate doctrine. People have grown accustomed to looking upon the first congregations, with their fullness of the Spirit and boldness of faith, as altogether perfect, an unattainable ideal, a company made up of nothing but saints and martyrs, a peculiar divine wonder-time, and so forth; and then, with apparent right, they have concluded: “We cannot expect to be like the first Christians.” Thereby they have found an excuse for the crying contrast between the manifold dead mass-congregations among us and the apostolic congregations, and in part have further quieted themselves with the fleshly, self-satisfied thought that “it was another matter in former days,” and “that we must be thankful that our congregations are as good as they are”; whereas the truth is this, that if the congregation is the work of God’s Spirit, then our congregations too ought at least to become just as living and vigorous, just as bold in faith and patient, just as self-sacrificing and missionary as in the first Christian age.

For what do we find in this first apostolic age? First, Ananias and Sapphira and quarrels about food, as has already been mentioned. Next, that false teachings, grounded in the current Phariseeism and the fleshly unwillingness to let oneself be saved by grace alone, to be justified by faith without the works of the Law, quite early stole into the congregation in Jerusalem and outside it.

In the Asiatic congregations this tendency was carried much further, in that they set up a peculiar worship of God (the worship of angels) and an exaggerated asceticism or self-denial, which in their opinion was especially meritorious, in place of faith and grace alone (Col. 2:18ff.; 1 Tim. 4:1ff.). On the other side, in many places people began to set worldly wisdom, long genealogies, and profane fables in place of the simple Gospel (1 Tim. 4:7; 6:20), to deny or explain away the resurrection (2 Tim. 2:18; 1 Cor. 15:12), to explain the Lord’s return for fleshly advantage (2 Thess. 3:6), and to use Christian freedom for gain or as an occasion for the flesh (1 Tim. 6:5).

But not only that. Already early the spirit of falsehood and the lust to rule began to creep into the congregations. In the first Thessalonian epistle, which is the earliest letter we have from Paul, he warns against such as either spoke in his name or even sent false letters in his name (2 Thess. 2:2), for which reason at the end of the epistle he expressly calls attention to the way in which he writes his name. In Asia, he says that “all had turned away from him,” and besides this he mentions explicitly two persons, Phygellus and Hermogenes. Demas forsook him out of love for the world, and Alexander the coppersmith had done him much evil (2 Tim. 4:10 and 14). Compare with this 2 Tim. 3:1ff.; likewise 2 Peter and the Epistle of Jude. John says that Diotrephes, who likes to be among the foremost, would not even acknowledge him, but refused to receive the brothers and cast them out of the congregation! (3 John 9ff.) And just as Jesus had done with His disciples, so Peter finds it necessary (1 Pet. 5:1ff.) to admonish the elders both against serving for the sake of gain and against lording it over the flock.

And this is not all. In almost every single epistle there are warnings not only against the above-mentioned perhaps somewhat finer spiritual sins, but also against all manner of gross vices, such as were readily carried over from a surrounding heathen world corrupted to the utmost, to which the members of the congregation had every temptation to fall back. (Eph. 5:3ff.; Col. 3:5ff.; 1 Thess. 4:3ff.; Gal. 5:13ff.; etc.)

For to Paul and the other New Testament writers the congregation does not stand forth as something loose and indefinite, something invisible and general, believers wherever they might be found, without regard to their outward visible union and assembly; rather, it stands forth as a distinct circle consisting of men and women, slaves and free, rich and poor, families and children, separated from the rest of the world by this their outward ordering and by the Word and the Spirit by which they had been separated, and in which nevertheless Satan had been able to produce all the deviations and defections and sins mentioned above.

In almost all the epistles Paul sends greetings to named persons in the congregation, and sends greetings not only from individual persons but from whole congregations, just as he also mentions Gospel work as proceeding from the individual congregations as a whole. In the Epistle to the congregation in Rome, where he himself had not personally been, he nevertheless mentions in his greeting by name so many that their number, including the greeted families, can scarcely be put at less than fifty persons. And if he had such knowledge of the individual members of a relatively unfamiliar congregation, how closely must he not have known the individual members in the congregations he himself had founded, such as, for example,

The Corinthian Congregation?

We will therefore dwell briefly on this.
We have preserved for us in God’s Word two epistles from Paul to this congregation, for which he expresses such heartfelt love and such deep concern.

What, then, was the condition in this congregation?

There were many and great faults. First factionalism: one held to Paul, one to Cephas, and so forth. There were gross sins of immorality, and worse still, a lack of that spirit of holiness which exercises discipline against open ungodliness. There were greed and litigiousness, so common both among Jews and Gentiles. There was carelessness with respect to the holy Lord’s Supper, and class-pride, so that one held himself better than another, drank himself drunk at the meals, and let the poor brothers go hungry. There was spiritual pride over their great spiritual gifts, for which reason they also esteemed the more conspicuous gifts, such as tongues, above the simpler but more necessary gifts, prophecy, mutual brotherly admonition, and above all love. False fleshly teachings about the resurrection had crept in, and lukewarmness in Christian works of mercy. They allowed themselves to be led astray by false intruding brothers, who carried on intrigues against Paul and sought to destroy the fruit of his labor. Therefore he speaks sharp words of punishment over them and says: “I fear that, when I come, perhaps I shall not find you such as I wish, and that I shall be found by you such as you do not wish: that there be strife, jealousy, wrath, quarrels, slanders, whisperings, puffed-up pride, disorders; that when I come again, my God shall humble me among you, and I shall mourn over many who formerly sinned and have not repented of the uncleanness and fornication and sensuality which they have committed” (2 Cor. 12:20-21).

“I fear that, as the serpent deceived Eve by his cunning, so your minds shall be corrupted and led away from faithfulness to Christ” (2 Cor. 11:3). “Some of you have no knowledge of God; I say this to your shame” (1 Cor. 15:34), “and many among you are weak and sickly, and a whole multitude sleep” (1 Cor. 11:30).

And, as in Corinth, so there seems more or less, to judge from the exhortations of the apostles and from the seven epistles in Revelation, to have been great deficiencies in all the other congregations as well.

And yet Paul writes to this same Corinthian congregation: “I thank my God always for you, for the grace of God which is given you in Christ Jesus, that in Him you have been made rich in all things, in all utterance and knowledge, even as the testimony of Christ has been confirmed in you, so that you come behind in no gift of grace, as you wait for the revelation of our Lord Jesus Christ” (1 Cor. 1:4ff.).

And in the second Epistle to the Corinthians he says: “Make room for us! We have wronged no one, corrupted no one, taken advantage of no one. I do not say this to condemn you; for I said before that you are in our hearts, to die together and to live together. I have great confidence in you, I boast much of you, I am filled with comfort, I have an exceedingly great joy in all our affliction. For though I grieved you with that epistle, I do not regret it, though I did regret it; for I see that the epistle, though only for a time, grieved you. Now I rejoice, not that you were grieved, but that you were grieved unto repentance; for you were grieved according to God, so that you might in no way suffer loss through us. Therefore we have been comforted by your comfort; but we rejoiced far more over Titus’ joy, because his spirit has been refreshed by you all. For if I have boasted much of you to him, I have not been put to shame; but just as we spoke all things in truth to you, so also our boasting before Titus has become truth, and he has an exceedingly great love for you when he remembers the obedience of you all, how you received him with fear and trembling.”

Such was the composition of the first apostolic congregations. There were vessels unto honor and vessels unto dishonor. They were like a Christian still yet in the world, surrounded and permeated by its sin and entanglement; they were neither perfect, nor had they attained it. But they pressed on after it; they had the life of God’s Spirit in them; they had within themselves an unceasing witness concerning sin and righteousness and judgment, so that they were not allowed to sink either into security in sin, or into comfort drawn from pure doctrine or from holy living, but kept themselves awake unto a daily sorrow and daily repentance unto God.

And thus they were enabled to carry on the work of the Gospel in the world, though in weakness, yet in inward sincerity, in the power and love of Christ’s Spirit; and although the congregations formed neither an episcopal church nor a priestly order, yet there was among the congregations in their mutual relation a manifestation or realization of the unity of the Church such as has perhaps scarcely ever existed since.
