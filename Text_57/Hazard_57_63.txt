STEP 1: HAZARD IDENTIFICATION

Lexical Hazards

* "fri forbindelse" does not mean merely "voluntary association" in a modern organizational sense; it carries ecclesiological force: a non-coercive, spiritually grounded relation among congregations.
* "fri Menighed / fri Menigheder" does not simply mean politically independent churches; the text explicitly distinguishes this from freedom from state control. It means congregations truly set free in Christ.
* "selvindlysende Sandheder" carries an ironic edge. It invokes what is supposedly obvious, only to show how difficult such a truth is in practice.
* "selvsagt" means self-evident or self-evidently true, not casual or merely customary.
* "Synder" and "Forbryder" must retain moral and forensic weight: sinner and criminal/offender. The argument is that salvation comes only by pardon.
* "benaades" is best rendered "be pardoned" rather than merely "forgiven," because the judicial image is essential.
* "den ene trange Vei" echoes biblical language of the narrow way and should retain spiritual exclusivity.
* "af Naade alene" must be rendered consistently as "by grace alone," preserving Lutheran doctrinal precision.
* "Forudsætningen" here means the indispensable prior condition, not a casual assumption.
* "Overherredømme," "Overformynderskab" indicate domination, tutelage, guardianship from above; these must not be flattened.
* "isandhed" means truly, in truth, indeed.
* "frigjorte" means liberated/freed, with strong evangelical force.
* "gjennemtrængte, beherskede og drevne" forms a deliberate triad: penetrated/pervaded, governed/ruled, and driven by the Spirit.
* "vækkes, levendegjøres og frigjøres" is a doctrinal progression: awakened, made alive, and set free.
* "Frikirken er derfor kun, idet den uafbrudt bliver" means the Free Church exists only in continuous becoming. The phrase must retain its dynamic, unfinished ecclesiological force.
* "udvortes" is crucial throughout. It means outward/external/visible, often contrasted with the spiritual essence.
* "stykkevis" echoes 1 Corinthians 13. It means in part, fragmentarily, partially.
* "fordringsfulde Fornuft" means demanding, exacting reason; not merely "human thought."
* "Tankeexperiment" should remain "thought-experiment," preserving the polemical tone.
* "Fædrene" means the Church Fathers or ecclesial forefathers depending on context; here the resonance includes both historical fathers and authoritative tradition.
* "Enhedsmetoder" means methods of unity, not merely administrative models.
* "strander" carries the image of running aground, foundering.
* "Statholder" is vicar/governor. In context, "Christ’s vicar" is the most natural ecclesiastical rendering.
* "forfal sket" means falsified/corrupted/adulterated; "corrupted" fits doctrinal context.
* "udslettet" means nearly erased or obliterated.
* "Statskirkeprincipet / Territorialprincipet / Synodalprincipet" are technical ecclesiological terms and must be rendered consistently as principles.
* "Verdslighed" means worldliness/secularization; here "complete worldliness" carries the charge.
* "Fritænker" is freethinker, not merely nonconformist.
* "Vækkelsesarbeide" means revival work or awakening work; it should retain revivalist theological resonance.
* "forliges" means reconciled or brought into accommodation; here it suggests a false peace.
* "Herred," "Kommune," "Township," "County" should retain the escalating concreteness of civil units.
* "udenpaa klistret Mærke" is vivid and contemptuous: a label pasted on from the outside.
* "Samfundsprincipet" in this setting means the denominational or associational principle, not "society" in a generic sense.
* "Selvstændighed" means self-standing independence, not modern autonomy detached from doctrine.
* "Gjennemgangsled" means an intermediate link or transitional stage.
* "Skraaplan" means slippery slope or inclined plane. "Slippery slope" is idiomatic but should not sound colloquial; "inclined plane" is too literal. A careful rendering may retain the image.
* "Stattefundament" appears to mean support/foundation or underpinning; the sense is that the congregation is reduced to a financial base for the synod.
* "Indbegrebet" means totality/sum/comprehensive whole.
* "Stand" means estate/order/class, especially clerical estate.
* "veiet og funden for let" echoes Daniel 5 and should preserve biblical resonance: weighed and found wanting / too light.
* "Sandhedens Pille og Grundvold" is scriptural and should be rendered in a way that preserves the citation’s gravity.
* "nedigjennem Tiderne" means down through the ages.
* "guddommeligt berettigede" means divinely warranted/authorized.
* "Oplysning og Veiledning" means illumination and guidance, not merely information.
* "tilveiebringes" means brought about, effected, brought into being.

Syntactic Hazards

* The prose relies on long cumulative periods in which definitions, qualifications, and contrasts unfold by clause upon clause. These should not be broken into short modern sentences without loss.
* The text uses semicolon logic repeatedly: an assertion is made, then deepened, qualified, or turned. This structure must be preserved.
* Numbered lists are integral to the argument and should remain visibly structured.
* Parenthetical scriptural citations interrupt but also authorize the flow; they must remain integrated, not detached as scholarly footnotes.
* The author often defines a term negatively before positively (for example, what "free congregations" do not mean, then what they do mean). This sequence is rhetorically important.
* Rhetorical questions should retain their pressure and not be turned into indirect discourse.
* Antithetical formulations recur: many and yet one; one and yet many; priest everything, congregation nothing; state everything, congregation nothing. These require exact parallel preservation.
* Repeated "idet den bliver" and similar dynamic expressions should remain rhythmically marked.
* Some paragraphs pivot from concession to rebuttal with "Men" and "for det første / for det andet." These pivots organize the theological reasoning and must stay prominent.

Theological Hazards

* The relation between sinner, crime, pardon, grace, and salvation is explicitly forensic and confessional; it must not be psychologized.
* Freedom is not political liberalism but freedom in Christ through the Gospel by the Spirit.
* Congregational freedom is inseparable from spiritual regeneration and the work of the Holy Spirit.
* The church is one holy catholic church, yet outwardly many. This paradox must remain doctrinally intact.
* The contrast between outward visible unity and inward spiritual reality is central; translations must not collapse one into the other.
* Critiques of episcopal, territorial, and synodal principles are not merely institutional preferences but theological judgments about the nature of the church.
* The phrase "the only outward form set by God for His kingdom on earth" is doctrinally decisive and must remain strong.
* The congregation as "pillar and ground/foundation of the truth" is scriptural and ecclesiologically central.
* "Christi Legeme" must be rendered "the body of Christ," not generalized.
* The insistence that church doctrine and order alike must be tested by the Word alone is a distinctly Lutheran claim and must remain explicit.
* The impossible becoming possible with God is not rhetorical flourish but a governing theological principle of the passage.

Voice Hazards

* The tone is solemn, argumentative, pastoral, and polemical at once. Any smoothing into neutral academic English would erase the sermonic force.
* The author speaks with controlled urgency, not emotional excess. The translation must keep the seriousness without melodrama.
* Irony appears in phrases like "self-evident truths" and "beautiful thought-experiment"; it must remain dry and cutting.
* The prose repeatedly presses consequence: if one seeks visible unity by rational means, the congregation is sacrificed. This pressure must remain morally sharp.
* Older Lutheran gravity is conveyed by repetition, parallel negations, and uncompromising contrasts. These must not be diluted.
* Appeals to Scripture are not ornamental. They function as decisive authority and must sound that way.

STEP 2: IDIOM ANALYSIS

* "selvindlysende Sandheder" means so-called self-evident truths. The phrase is partly ironic: what is obvious in principle proves nearly impossible in practice.
* "at frelses af Naade alene" is a confessional formula with direct Lutheran doctrinal force. "To be saved by grace alone" is the necessary rendering.
* "den ene trange Vei" likely echoes the biblical narrow way (Matthew 7:14), though adapted. Literal retention is preferable because the spiritual force depends on exclusivity.
* "Hvor Herrens Aand er, der er Frihed" is a direct scriptural citation from 2 Corinthians 3:17. A natural English equivalent is "Where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is freedom."
* "vi forstaar stykkevis og profeterer stykkevis" and "naar det fuldkomne kommer" echo 1 Corinthians 13:9-10. The established biblical phrasing "we know in part" is possible, but because the original says "understand," the translation should preserve the author’s wording while keeping the biblical cadence.
* "een hellig almindelig Kirke" echoes the creed. In English, "one holy catholic Church" best preserves doctrinal precision unless the surrounding context requires explanation. "Catholic" here is creedal, not Roman Catholic.
* "mange og dog en; en og dog mange" is a compressed ecclesiological paradox, reinforced by 1 Corinthians 10:17. It should remain aphoristic and balanced.
* "veiet og funden for let" echoes Daniel 5:27. In English, "weighed and found wanting" gives a natural biblical equivalent, though "found too light" is closer literally. Because the text’s resonance matters, "weighed and found wanting" is likely best.
* "Sandhedens Pille og Grundvold, den levende Guds Menighed" echoes 1 Timothy 3:15. English Bibles vary: "pillar and ground of the truth" or "pillar and foundation of the truth." Since the author’s resonance is older and doctrinal, "pillar and ground of the truth" better preserves the antique theological register without using archaic pronouns.
* "Hvad der er umuligt hos Menneskene, er muligt for Gud" echoes Luke 18:27. The natural English equivalent is "What is impossible with men is possible with God."
* "Guds Tempel, hans Menighed, er hellig ... hvilket I er" echoes 1 Corinthians 3:17. The scriptural gravity depends on preserving apposition and the final direct application.
* "ikke med Magt eller Kraft, men alene ved Guds Aand" strongly echoes Zechariah 4:6. The English should preserve the contrast: "not by might or by power, but only by the Spirit of God," though because the Danish says God’s Spirit, not "my Spirit," the wording should follow the author.
* "gaa baade Fædre og Tradition forbi" means to pass beyond Fathers and Tradition in order to seek truth in Scripture itself. This is not contempt for history but a Reformation ordering of authorities.
* "det gamle Spørgsmaal: Er I klogere end alle vore Fædre?" carries polemical familiarity. It must sound like a stock objection, not a fresh inquiry.
* "det umulige" is not merely the impractical; it is that which reason judges impossible, yet faith undertakes because God can do it.
* "kun, idet den bliver" is a compact theological idiom of dynamic becoming. Literal retention is important because it asserts an unfinished, living process rather than a completed institutional fact.

STEP 3: ARGUMENT STRUCTURE IDENTIFICATION

* Thesis: If congregations are truly free, the connection among them must also be free.
* Immediate paradox: This is self-evident in principle yet extraordinarily difficult, even seemingly impossible, in practice.
* Illustrative analogy: Just as the sinner can only be saved by pardon and grace alone, yet human reason devises many other ways, so too the church resists the simple but narrow way of free connection among free congregations.
* First main reason for difficulty: the prerequisite is not merely institutional independence but truly free congregations in Christ, inwardly liberated by the Gospel and the Spirit.
* Practical consequence: The Free Church’s task is therefore immense, since such congregations come into being only as they are awakened, enlivened, and liberated by the Holy Spirit.
* Second main reason for difficulty: even if the prerequisite is present, the execution appears self-contradictory, because the church is both many and one.
* Clarification of the problem: Any connection among congregations seeks to present an outward visible form of this unity, but all earthly realization is only partial.
* Rational objection: Reason demands a complete, visible, sensible unity and therefore dismisses free connection among free congregations as a beautiful but unattainable ideal.
* Historical survey: Church history shows that Christians have repeatedly sought more practical and rational methods to realize unity.
* Three methods named: episcopal principle, territorial/state-church principle, synodal principle.
* Evaluation of all three: each succeeds in producing an outward visible unity, but only at the expense of the congregation’s freedom and individuality.
* Detailed critique of episcopal principle: it culminates in the papacy; visible unity is achieved, but doctrine is corrupted and the congregation nearly erased.
* Detailed critique of territorial principle: it produces worldliness; when church and state are reconciled, the spiritual essence of the congregation dissolves into a civil unit.
* Detailed critique of synodal principle: it appears better because it can acknowledge congregational independence in theory, but in practice it elevates synod/association into a higher church-form and reduces the congregation to a subordinate stage, eventually empowering clergy as the effective bearers of unity.
* Summative judgment: all these methods have been tried for nearly nineteen hundred years and have been weighed and found wanting, because they replace the living congregation with human organizations.
* Positive turn: therefore serious Christians, especially those building a Lutheran congregation anew, must attempt what reason calls impossible.
* Governing theological answer: the congregation is God’s work; it cannot be built or united by might or power, but only by God’s Spirit.
* Anticipated objection from tradition: Are you wiser than all the Fathers?
* First response: the same question was asked of Luther; one Word of God outweighs all the Fathers; doctrine and order alike must be tested by Scripture alone.
* Second response: the New Testament itself provides sufficient knowledge of the church in both divine essence and historical reality.
* Concluding transition: therefore two questions arise concerning the continuity between New Testament congregations and present congregations, and whether a similar unity of connection can be brought about today.

STEP 5: VOICE INTENSITY PRESERVATION

* The translation must preserve the severity of the critique of institutional church forms. The author is not neutrally comparing models; he is warning that each humanly rationalized form eventually destroys the congregation.
* The analogy of the sinner saved only by pardon must retain its hard exclusivity; the human mind seeks many escape-routes from grace alone.
* Phrases like "the priest has become everything, the congregation nothing" and "the state becomes everything" must remain stark.
* The repeated insistence that the true congregation is God’s own work and cannot be produced by force must remain absolute.
* The objection from the Fathers must sound like a real challenge, and the reply must carry confessional firmness.
* The close should feel like a transition into further doctrinal inquiry, not a soft conclusion.

STEP 11: ACADEMIC TRANSLATION (VOICE-PRESERVING, SVERDRUP PROFILE)

Free Connection among Free Congregations.

Opening Address at the Free Church’s First Meeting,
by Professor Sven Oftedal.

[Report of the Annual Meeting of the Lutheran Free Church, 1897, p. 62ff.]

It may well be maintained as one of the so-called self-evident truths, a matter taken for granted, that if the congregations are free, then the mutual connection among them must also become a free connection.

Nevertheless, or rather precisely because it is self-evident, it is so exceedingly difficult, not to say impossible, to carry out in practice.

It is self-evident that a sinner, because he is a criminal, can be saved only by being pardoned; and yet how difficult, indeed impossible! And how many other ways has not human reason and theology devised in order to avoid this one narrow way, to be saved by grace alone!

Free connection among free congregations is extraordinarily difficult to realize:

* 1. On account of the prerequisite.
* 2. On account of the apparent self-contradiction in its execution.

The prerequisite is, namely, free congregations, or a true freedom of the congregation.

By free congregations we understand, in this connection, not congregations that are freed from the supremacy of the state, or even from the tutelage of the priesthood or of the papacy, but congregations that are in truth free in Christ, set free in their individual members by the Gospel, pervaded, governed, and driven by the Spirit of God according to the word: "Where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is freedom" (2 Cor. 3:17).

If congregations of that sort are the prerequisite for the aim here under consideration, then anyone who knows anything of the condition of our congregations will at once understand not only how great, but how difficult, the task lying before the Free Church is.

Only in the same measure as the congregations are awakened, made alive, and set free by the Holy Spirit and power of God can there gradually be any question of establishing a free connection among them. The Free Church therefore is only as it continually becomes.

But even if the prerequisite is present, the execution is equally difficult, because it involves an apparent contradiction. God’s congregation is, namely, at the same time that it is many, yet always only one. We believe one holy catholic Church. Connection among the congregations always aims to present an outward visible form of this unity.

But here we are met by the fact that everything in the world is "in part": "we understand in part and we prophesy in part; and only when the perfect comes shall that which is in part be done away" (1 Cor. 13:9-10). Therefore freedom also, true spiritual freedom, is always in part; and naturally every free connection is also in part; it too is only as it becomes, and therefore does not satisfy the demanding reason’s claim for an outward, sensible, visible, and perfect unity. The Church cannot at one and the same time be both many and one, says reason.

"Free connection among free congregations," as a presentation of the unity of the Church, is therefore only a beautiful thought-experiment, an unattainable ideal, people say; it is not only a difficulty, but an outright impossibility.

The history of the Church therefore shows us that the "Fathers" have always had to seek more practical and rational ways by which the connection among the congregations might realize the great, inescapable goal: the unity of the Church.

These rational methods of unity, no doubt often forced forth by historical conditions, may be gathered under three great groups:

* 1. The episcopal principle.
* 2. The territorial principle.
* 3. The synodal principle.

By all these methods one succeeds in presenting an outward, unmistakable form of the unity of the Church, but it always takes place at the expense of the congregation’s freedom and individuality. They all run aground on the contradiction: many and yet one; one and yet many (cf. 1 Cor. 10:17).

The episcopal principle, which in reality makes the congregation identical with the bishop, leads in its consistent development to the papacy; that is to say, to the unity of the Church represented by the highest bishop, Christ’s vicar. Although the Catholic Church, with its personal visible head and its fully developed organization, has incontestably gone farthest in presenting the unity of the Church on an admirable and almost world-embracing scale, yet we Lutherans acknowledge that this has taken place at the expense both of doctrine and of the congregation. The doctrine of God’s Word has been corrupted, and the congregation almost altogether obliterated. The priest has become everything, the congregation nothing. The existence of our Lutheran Church is precisely a serious and definite protest, grounded in the Word of God, against this principle.

The state-church principle, which is also called the territorial principle because the boundaries of the congregation are determined by geographical divisions or territories, has early been tried both in Catholic and in Protestant lands. Every time it has been tried, it has led to complete worldliness. We have seen its fruits in our old fatherland, where a hundred years ago almost every single priest was a rationalist or freethinker, and Hauge, on account of his revival work, was cast into prison. When God and the world are apparently reconciled, it is God who is excluded; when the congregation and the state are reconciled or united, then there does indeed come about as complete a union as human power and compulsion can produce, but also in this form of the unity of God’s Church it is the worldly state that becomes everything; the congregation becomes nothing but an outward form; it is in reality dissolved according to its spiritual essence and passes over into becoming equivalent to a district or a municipality, a township or county, in short a purely worldly organization with the Word and the Sacraments as a label pasted on from the outside.

The synodal principle has also, from the earliest time and right down to our own days, been tried in both Catholic and Protestant lands. It is what in this country we are most familiar with under the name of the associational principle. It has great advantages over the two preceding. In theory, and even in constitutions, it can strongly emphasize and acknowledge the independence of the individual congregation. But since this principle too aims at an outward, visible, and organized unity of the Church and seeks to realize this in the "synod" or the "association," a consistent development seems unavoidably to lead, little by little, to setting up the "synod" or the "association" as a higher sort of congregation, a form of unity of all the congregations belonging to the association. Thereby the congregation has already lost its place as the only outward form appointed by God for His kingdom on earth; it has been reduced to being an intermediate stage leading to a higher form of God’s Church, fashioned by men.

Once the development has entered upon this slippery slope, though in many gradations, congregations will unceasingly have to withdraw more and more, until at last they are even reduced to a mere supporting foundation for the financial operations of the "association." The synod, as the totality and unity of all the congregations belonging under it, will in the course of development come to present the outward form of this higher unity in the priests, who will then gradually and involuntarily regard themselves individually as special members of the congregation, placed higher by ordination, and in their totality, as an "estate" or "priesthood," consider themselves the actual representatives of the Church and of its unity. It is here that the synodal principle in its development will meet the papacy, no doubt on a smaller scale, but on no less effective a one. Here too the congregation is sacrificed for outward priestly church-unity.

These different ways of presenting the unity of the congregation, in order to avoid the self-evident and simple, but for human reason impossible thing: the preservation of freedom both for the individual congregation and for their mutual connection, have now for nearly nineteen hundred years again and again been tried and weighed and each time found wanting, because in the course of their development they have all blurred or corrupted "the pillar and ground of the truth, the congregation of the living God," and have set up their own organizations in its place, partly priestly, partly civil.

For serious-minded, independent Christians, therefore, and especially for us, who have to build a Lutheran congregation as if anew in this country, there remains nothing else than to attempt the "impossible," according to the word that stands written: What is impossible with men is possible with God. "God’s temple, His congregation, is holy," says Scripture, and adds: "which you are." The congregation is not something human; it is God’s work; it can therefore neither be built nor united in its multiplicity into one, whether by might or by power, but only by the Spirit of God. He alone is the one who can establish a "free connection among free congregations."

It is true that the history of the Church after the apostolic period shows us that the Church’s foremost and most earnest leaders, the Fathers, down through the ages, have almost all regarded one of the three above-named principles as divinely warranted in the work of presenting the unity of the Church outwardly; and with apparent justice the old question may also be raised here: "Are you wiser than all our Fathers?"

But first, the same question was put to Luther. Therefore it is true Lutheran doctrine that one Word of God weighs more than all the Fathers. Both the congregation’s doctrine and its order, two things which at many points are inseparable, must be tested by the Word of God and by the Word of God alone. Fathers, history, tradition may be of inestimable value for illumination and guidance in spiritual matters. But when it concerns the very pillar and ground of the truth, the congregation of the living God, the body of Christ, its essence and unity, then it is our duty and our right, most of all as Lutheran Christians, to pass beyond both Fathers and Tradition and to seek in the Word itself the knowledge and strength that we need.

Second, the New Testament gives all desirable instruction about the congregation both according to its divine essence and according to its historical reality.

There now therefore present themselves two questions:

* 1. Was the congregation, as it meets us within the period of the New Testament, of the same kind and essence as the congregation now?
* 2. Was there among these New Testament free congregations any unity of connection, and can a similar one be brought about among our congregations?
